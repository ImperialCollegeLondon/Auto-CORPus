Abstract

Study Objective:

There are few studies on gene-environment interactions with obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). Our study aimed to explore genetic polymorphisms associated with OSA using genome-wide association (GWA) data and evaluate the effects of relevant polymorphisms on the association between risk factors, including obesity and alcohol consumption, and OSA. We also investigated on these associations in relation to cerebral white matter hyperintensities (WMH) on magnetic resonance images.

Design:

A cross-sectional design.

Setting:

A polysomnography study embedded in a population-based cohort from the Korean Genome Epidemiology Study was conducted in 2011–2013.

Participants:

1,763 participants aged 48–78 years.

Results:

251 individuals were identified to have OSA with an apnea-hypopnea index ≥ 15. A common polymorphism of neuregulin-1 gene (NRG1), rs10097555, was selected as the most suggestive locus associated with OSA (P value < 10−5) based on the results of GWA analysis in a matched case-control subsample (n = 470). Among 1,763 participants, we found that the presence of the NRG1 polymorphism is inversely associated with OSA (P value < 0.01) even after taking into account potential risk factors; the multivariate odds ratio (95% confidence interval) for the mutant alleles was 0.57 (0.39–0.82) compared with the wild-type. We observed that this association is modified by alcohol consumption (P < 0.05), not by obesity. We also observed that WMH are positively associated with OSA independent of the NRG1 polymorphism and alcohol consumption (P < 0.05).

Conclusions:

These findings suggest that the neuregulin-1 gene (NRG1) may be involved in the etiological mechanisms of obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) and that carriers of a particular NRG1 mutation may be less likely to have OSA if they do not drink alcoholic beverages.

INTRODUCTION

The etiology of obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is not fully understood; however, some pathophysiologic mechanisms have been proposed. These include anatomical narrowness of the upper airway (UA), abnormal pharyngeal collapsibility, and ventilatory instability during sleep.1,2 Additionally, many factors are hypothesized to influence the mechanisms of OSA.1–3 According to previous reports, old age, male sex, smoking, alcohol consumption, sedative use, and obesity are associated with increased collapsibility of the UA.2,3 Furthermore, obesity is associated with unstable ventilatory control as well as narrow UA lumens because of fat deposition in the neck.2,3 Heritability or genetic susceptibility is considered an important factor underlying the etiology,2,3 but causative genes that influence the development of OSA are still undetermined. Most population-based genetic studies for OSA adopted a candidate gene approach, which has been criticized because it commonly considers a limited number of genes and polymorphisms.4 Two genetic studies have comprehensively evaluated candidate genes, including genes related to facial anatomy, ventilator control, inflammation, sleep, obesity, and OSA-related diseases.5,6 However, their findings on significant or potentially meaningful genes that may affect OSA susceptibility were conflicting.5,6 A genome-wide association (GWA) study for OSA may provide further information beyond candidate genes proposed in previous studies. However, data from such study design are limited at the present. Moreover, there is little data available on gene-environment interactions in associations with OSA.

Recently, we have reported data on a positive association between OSA and cerebral white matter hyperintensities (WMH) on magnetic resonance images (MRI).7 Earlier neuroimaging studies, but not all, have shown that patients with OSA are likely to have brain structural abnormalities, such as reduced hippocampal volume or focal reductions in gray matter.8–11 A major hypothesis explaining these associations is that OSA may cause hypoxic brain damage via repeated intermittent apnea and hypopnea.8–10 To the best of our knowledge, no study has reported genetic effects on the association between OSA and brain structural abnormalities.

The objectives of this study were to explore GWA data for an association with OSA, examine OSA severity and risk factors according to genotypes of the most meaningful single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP), investigate interactions between the SNP genotypes and risk factors (gene-environment interactions) on OSA, and further evaluate genetic modification in the association between OSA and WMH lesions by the selected SNP.

METHODS

Study Design and Population

We carried out a cross-sectional study embedded in a population-based cohort included in the Korean Genome Epidemiology Study. Detailed information on the ongoing prospective cohort study conducted in an urban community is available elsewhere.12 Briefly, the cohort consists of 5,015 male and female adults aged 40–69 years at the beginning of the study; the baseline period was between June 18, 2001, and January 29, 2003. All participants, who were Korean and had the same ethnic background, completed a comprehensive health examination and on-site interviews at the Korea University Ansan Hospital. In the interview, participants answered a questionnaire to provide demographic information, medical history, health conditions, family disease history, and lifestyle details. Participants have been followed up biennially with a scheduled site visit for similar examinations and interviews.

Among the cohort members, 1,763 participants underwent polysomnography (PSG) during the period between February 22, 2011, and February 28, 2013 and were included in the present study. In further examinations, 92.3% of the study participants completed echocardiogram, and 70.4% of them underwent brain MRI.

The Human Subjects Review Committee at the Korea University Ansan Hospital approved the study protocols as well as an informed consent form, which each participant signed every site visit.

PSG and OSA

To determine the presence and severity of OSA, an unattended overnight PSG recording was conducted either at the patient's home or at the sleep laboratory of Korea University Ansan Hospital by using a portable monitoring device (Embletta X-100; Embla Systems, Broomfield, CO, USA). This device consists of 1-channel electroencephalogram, electro-oculogram, chin muscle electromyogram, eletrocardiogram, and pulse oximetry, as well as a pressure transducer air flow sensor and a chest and abdominal respiratory movement sensor. A trained sleep technologist connected the device to the patient at bedtime, collected an unattended overnight recording on the following morning, and manually scored the results of PSG according to standard criteria.13 Apnea was defined as > 90% reduction in airflow from baseline for ≥ 10 s, and hypopnea was defined as ≥ 30% reduction in airflow from baseline accompanied by ≥ 4% decrease in oxygen saturation. We obtained the apnea-hypopnea index (AHI), which is the average number of apnea and hypopnea events per sleep hour, and classified OSA as mild (5 ≤ AHI < 15), moderate (15 ≤ AHI < 30), or severe (AHI ≥ 30). In the present study, we focused on the moderate-to-severe OSA cases with AHI ≥ 15.

Brain MRI and WMH

Within a week after the PSG evaluation, each participant underwent a brain MRI examination at the Central Hospital (Gyeonggi Province, Republic of Korea) with a 1.5T imaging device (GE Medical Systems, Waukesha, WI, USA). To reduce potential bias, a trained radiologist blinded to the PSG results as well as to the genetic and clinical information of participants evaluated WMH on T2-weighted fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) images. Detailed information on the parameters for the FLAIR images and the definition of WMH has been provided in an earlier study.7

GWA Analysis in a Matched Case-Control Subsample

Information on the preparation of genomic samples, genotyping method, and quality control procedures for geno-typing data is available in a previous GWA study14 and the supplemental material. On the basis of the original GWA data including 352,228 SNPs, we included only cohort members who underwent a polysomnography during the period between February 22, 2011, and February 28, 2013, at the Korea University Ansan Hospital; thus data for 1,763 participants were available for GWA analysis. To explore genetic loci associated with OSA efficiently, we selected moderate-to-severe OSA cases and their matched controls. The selection of a matched case-control subsample (case-to-control = 1:1) was conducted using an automated matching procedure in the SAS software (SAS 9.1.3, SAS Institute, Cary, NC, USA). According to the procedure, controls were randomly selected from the eligible participants (n = 979) who did not have OSA (AHI < 5) and whose genetic data were available, and then each of them was matched by age (± 2 years) and sex for each index case. This procedure was repeated until the maximum number of a sub-sample was obtained. We did not select more than one control for each case because the matching criterion was not fulfilled for all sets. Thus, a total of 470 participants were selected for a subsample. The characteristics of this subsample are presented in Table 1. In GWA analysis for the subsample, we used genetic data of 319,517 SNPs after evaluating SNP call rates (> 95%), the Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE) test (P > 0.0001), and minor allele frequency (MAF) (> 5%).

Potential Risk Factors

Information on potential risk factors such as age, sex, smoking status, and alcohol consumption was collected through questionnaire-based interviews. In addition, anthropometric and echocardiographic data were collected through a comprehensive health examination conducted by health professionals according to a standardized protocol. Height (cm) and body weight (kg) were measured to the nearest 0.1 cm or 0.1 kg without footwear and body mass index (BMI, kg/m2) was calculated. Left ventricular ejection fraction % (LVEF %) was obtained from the echocardiogram.

Statistical Analysis

For GWA analysis, we conducted logistic regression analysis using PLINK software (version 1.06, http://pngu.mgh.harvard.edu/purcell/plink/) and obtained unadjusted and BMI-adjusted odds ratios (ORs) and its 95% CI. Three genetic models, such as additive, dominant, and recessive models, were applied to analysis. On the basis of association results that showed the smallest P value, a Manhattan plot and a Q-Q plot were depicted using the Haploview software (version 4.1, http://www.broad.mit.edu/mpg/haploview/) and the R statistical software package (version 2.9.2). For multiple testing corrections, we used Bonferroni adjustment. We also examined linkage disequilibrium (LD) with D′ between all pairs of biallelic SNPs using imputed data, which were generated on the basis of the Japanese and Chinese (JPT/ CHB) HapMap reference (http://hapmap.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). The LD map was created using the Haploview software (version 4.1, http://www.broad.mit.edu/mpg/haploview/).

To evaluate genetic effects, we selected a relevant SNP on the basis of association results that showed the smallest P value. We compared potential risk factors of OSA and the presence of WMH across the genotypes of a selected SNP using ANOVA and χ2 tests. To analyze associations between OSA and potential risk factors, we conducted logistic regression analysis and obtained ORs of moderate-to-severe OSA with 95% CI. We presented multivariate ORs associated with potential risk factors. In multivariate models, we also evaluated an interaction term between the genetic polymorphism and each of risk factors. Among risk factors, age, BMI, and neck circumference were fit as continuous variables and other variables were entered as categorical variables in the models. In further analyses to estimate ORs for WMH, we treated OSA, the genetic polymorphism, and risk factors as independent variables and WMH as a dependent variable after excluding missing values of WMH. All testing was based on a 2-sided level of significance (P < 0.05) and was conducted using the SAS software (SAS 9.1.3, SAS Institute, Cary, NC, USA).

RESULTS

Table 1 shows the characteristics of the subsample for GWA analysis. Moderate-to-severe OSA cases showed greater BMI, waist and hip circumference ratio, and neck circumference and higher prevalence of WMH diagnosis than controls. Results of GWA analysis according to the genetic models are presented in the Tables S1 and S2 (supplemental material). Although we could not find significant SNPs, we observed some suggestive intron SNPs, such as rs10097555, rs6984617, and rs7001605, even after the adjustment of BMI (Table S2). The range of P values for these SNPs was 5.4 × 10−6 through 9.9 × 10−6 and 2.6 × 10−6 through 6.0 × 10−6 before and after adjusting for BMI, respectively (Tables S1 and S2). Because the association testing under an additive model showed the smallest P values, a Manhattan plot and a Q-Q plot are presented for this model. On the basis of the Q-Q plot and an inflation factor (lambda = 1.006), it is considered that population stratification was unlikely in the findings (Figure S1, supplemental material). The 3 suggestive SNPs were observed on chromosome 8 (Figure 1), in particular on neuregulin-1 gene (NRG1), and were in high LD (D′ = 0.99) (Figure 2). Thus, we selected rs10097555, as the most suggestive SNP associated with OSA, for further analyses with data for 1,763 participants. In this population, the association testing of GWA data under an additive model showed 4.3 × 10−4 as a P value for rs10097555.

We calculated descriptive statistics and compared the characteristics of 1,763 participants according to the NRG1 rs10097555 genotypes (Table 2). Among this population, 251 were diagnosed with moderate-to-severe OSA. Genotype frequencies were 0.73 for AA, 0.25 for AG, and 0.02 for GG. Compared with carriers of the A allele, those with GG were likely to have narrower neck circumference (P < 0.05).

Results for the associations of the NRG1 polymorphism and potential risk factors with moderate-to-severe OSA are presented in Table 3. Because no case was observed among carriers with GG, we pooled data of those with the G allele for further analyses. Potential risk factors, such as age, sex, BMI, neck circumference, and alcohol consumption were taken into account in the multivariate model. Carriers with the G allele showed a 43% reduction in the multivariate OR (95% CI: 0.39–0.82) of moderate-to-severe OSA versus individuals without OSA (non-OSA cases; Table 3). Age, BMI, and neck circumference were also significantly associated with moderate-to-severe OSA. When non-OSA and mild OSA cases were combined and contrasted with moderate-to-severe OSA, the multivariate OR was 0.63 (95% CI: 0.45–0.88) for carriers with the G allele compared with those with the AA allele (data not shown). Multivariate logistic regression analyses were performed to evaluate whether interactions between the NRG1 polymorphism and potential risk factors on OSA are significant. Alcohol consumption was found to modify the association between the NRG1 polymorphism and OSA (P < 0.05) after taking into account other risk factors. These results did not change after further adjustment of LVEF % (data not shown).

In addition, we evaluated the associations of WMH with OSA, the NRG1 polymorphism, and potential risk factors. The NRG1 polymorphism was not associated with WMH whereas moderate-to-severe OSA was significantly associated with WMH independent of age. Moderate-to-severe OSA cases showed a 59% increased multivariate OR (95% CI: 1.09–2.31) of WMH (Table S3, supplemental material). However, a significantly increased OR was observed in individuals with moderate-to-severe OSA carrying the AA allele only; they showed a 56% higher OR (95% CI: 1.02–2.41) for WMH than did those with the AA allele who were free of OSA. Compared with the reference group, patients with OSA and the G allele did not show a significant increase in the OR of WMH. The interaction between the NRG1 polymorphism and OSA severity on WMH was not significant (data not shown).

DISCUSSION

In a population-based cross-sectional study, we collected data on the severity of OSA by using overnight PSG as well as on the presence of WMH by using brain MRI, and integrated these clinical data with GWA data. We found that signals on NRG1 as the most suggestive loci associated with OSA in GWA analysis and observed an inverse association between the mutant types of NRG1 rs10097555 polymorphism and OSA. We also observed that this association is modified by alcohol consumption and thus the beneficial effect of carrying the mutant types on OSA is presented in nondrinkers only. Regardless of the NRG1 polymorphism and alcohol consumption, however, OSA was significantly associated with WMH.

NRG1 encodes neuregulin 1 (NRG-1), which is one of four neuregulin (NRG) proteins that are ligands for ErbB receptor tyrosine kinases. NRGs are involved in growth, development, and maintenance of morphology and function of multiple organ systems.15,16 Initial studies on NRGs focused on the relation between the NRG/ErbB signaling axis and cancer development.17,18 A large portion of subsequent studies investigated the roles of NRG-1 and its encoding gene in the heart and nervous system and reported their associations with heart failure, schizophrenia, and Alzheimer disease.19–23 Furthermore, NRG-1 has recently been investigated as a new therapeutic target because it appears to be involved in the regenerative process following heart or nerve injury.24,25 For example, animal studies have shown the cardioprotective effects of an NRG1 mutation and NRG-1/ErbB signaling, which is enhanced via the injection of recombinant human NRG-1 (rhNRG-1).26,27 Although data from human studies are still limited, a clinical trial for the parenteral administration of rhNRG-1 demonstrated improvements in cardiac function in patients with chronic heart failure.28 The present study shows the first reported data on a protective effect of the NRG1 polymorphism on moderate-to-severe OSA in the general population. To the best of our knowledge, the association between NRG1 (or NRG-1) and OSA has not previously been addressed in human studies. In vitro studies support the association between NRG1 and OSA; the studies suggest that NRG-1/ErbB signaling may play a role in maintaining airway epithelial differentiation and airway epithelial barrier integrity.29,30 These potential biological functions of NRG-1 in the airway may be advantageous for carriers of the NRG1 polymorphism, in particular for those at risk of developing OSA, if the NRG1 polymorphism described is associated with enhancing NRG-1/ErbB signaling.

OSA is considered the most common type of sleep-related breathing disorders among adults. In pathophysiologic aspects of OSA, the pharynx is the site of partial or complete occlusion during sleep because of morphologic abnormality and/or collapsibility of its soft tissue.1,2 Schellenberg et al. found that the lateral pharyngeal structure is associated with UA narrowing independent of obesity among patients with OSA.31 In addition, Schwab et al. reported that these patients have enlarged lateral pharyngeal walls and UA soft tissues compared with normal controls.32 They also suggested that lateral pharyngeal wall and UA soft tissue structure are determined partially by genetic background unrelated to parapharyngeal fat pad volume.33 However, specific genes related to enlarged lateral pharyngeal walls have not been identified. On the basis of our findings, we postulate that NRG1 is a plausible candidate gene for OSA partly because of its involvement in UA narrowing due to pharyngeal morphology. In our findings, carriers with the NRG1 homozygous mutation had significantly smaller neck size than did those with other genotypes, and none of them had moderate-to-severe OSA. After adjustments for other risk factors of OSA including neck circumference, presence of the NRG1 mutation was found to be protective against the development of OSA. Our findings also suggest that this benefit may be attenuated among alcohol consumers because alcohol is likely to aggravate OSA due to increased UA resistance.34 In addition, our study showed that alcohol consumption modifies the association between OSA and the NRG1 polymorphism. Because there are no external data supporting this observation at this point, it would be interesting to test whether alcohol consumption down-regulates the expression of NRG1 and attenuates the protective effects of the NRG1 mutant on OSA.

A recent meta-analysis of association studies with candidate genes for OSA showed that polymorphisms of the tumor necrosis factor-α gene (TNFA), angiotensin I converting enzyme gene (ACE), and apolipoprotein E gene (APOE) have been studied more frequently than were polymorphisms of other genes.4 However, statistical significance was observed only for the association between the TNFA rs1800629 polymorphism and OSA.4 The Cleveland Family Study performed a candidate gene analysis for 1,080 SNPs from 52 candidate genes and suggested that the C-reactive protein gene (CRP), glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor gene (GDNF), and serotonin 2A receptor gene (HTR2A) may make a contribution to the development of OSA.5 In a subsequent study, which included almost 2,100 genes and analyzed data from multiple genetic studies, some novel genes such as pleckstrin gene (PLEK), prostaglandin E2 receptor gene (PTGER3), and lysophosphatidic acid receptor 1 gene (LPAR1) were found to be associated with OSA or AHI.6 These large-scale candidate gene studies stratified data analysis by African or European ancestry and showed ethnicity-specific findings.5,6 There is little data regarding genetic information for OSA among Asian populations. Because obesity is less prevalent in Asians than in other ethnicities,3 its impact on OSA may be undervalued, but facial or pharyngeal anatomy may play a greater role in genetic determinants for OSA.

Several neuroimaging studies have reported data on the association between OSA and brain structural abnormalities, such as focal gray matter loss or WMH.7–10 A potential mechanism underlying the association is that chronic intermittent hypoxia may cause cerebral neuronal damage via apoptotic cell death under oxidative stress. A recent study examined different features of frontal brain structure by the NRG1 rs6994992 variants in a general population.35 Thus we attempted to analyze associations among OSA, WMH, and the NRG1 polymorphism and observed that the NRG1 polymorphism was not associated with WMH. These data should be interpreted cautiously because the polymorphism that we selected is not the same one observed by the earlier study.35

Methodological strengths and limitations need to be considered in the interpretation of our findings. The present study used a large sample size from a general population, a wide range of genetic data, and clinical data from PSG and brain MRI examination. Study limitations include a lack of replicate data to support GWA findings and limited generalizability of the findings.

In summary, this study showed NRG1 as a novel gene associated with OSA. This study also showed potential interactions between NRG1 polymorphism and alcohol consumption in patients with OSA. Further investigations are warranted to confirm the role of NRG1 in the development of OSA and to explore gene-environment interaction mechanisms, particularly in diverse ethnicities.

SUPPLEMENTAL METHODS

Genome-Wide Association (GWA) Data

Genomic DNA was collected from blood samples and 500 ng of DNA was processed according to the Affymetrix Genome-wide Human SNP array 5.0 (Affymetrix, Inc., Santa Clara, CA, USA). Primarily, DNA samples from 10,004 individuals were obtained from the two cohorts; one of them consists of residents in Ansung and the other is for this study.1 After excluding 11 contaminated samples, genotype data were available for a total of 9,993 individuals. Further exclusion was made based on the following criteria: call rates below 96%, gender discrepancy compared with questionnaire data from the cohort studies, cancer cases that were identified when biospecimens were collected at baseline, or average pair-wise identity-by-state values > 0.8 as estimated values from first-degree relatives of Korean sib-pair samples. Remaining data included 8,842 men and women. Genotype calls were made for 500,568 SNPs using Bayesian Robust Linear Modeling using the Mahalanobis Distance Genotyping Algorithm. After discriminating SNPs on the standard protocol for quality control, 352,228 SNPs were remained and used for principal component analysis to confirm applicability of the Japanese and Chinese (JPT/CHB) HapMap reference (http://hapmap.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). Using such reference, SNP imputation was completed to generate imputed GWA data.1
