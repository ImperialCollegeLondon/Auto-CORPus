Abstract

OBJECTIVES

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is a chronic autoimmune disease with a strong genetic component. Our aim was to perform the first genome-wide association study on individuals from the Americas enriched for Native American heritage.

MATERIALS and METHODS

We analyzed 3,710 individuals from four countries of Latin America and the Unites States diagnosed with SLE and healthy controls. Samples were genotyped with the HumanOmni1 BeadChip. Data of out-of-study controls was obtained for the HumanOmni2.5. Statistical analyses were performed using SNPTEST and SNPGWA. Data was adjusted for genomic control and FDR. Imputation was done using IMPUTE2, and HiBAG for classical HLA alleles.

RESULTS

The IRF5-TNPO3 region showed the strongest association and largest odds ratio (OR) (rs10488631, Pgcadj = 2.61×10−29, OR = 2.12, 95% CI: 1.88–2.39) followed by the HLA class II on the DQA2-DQB1 loci (rs9275572, Pgcadj = 1.11 × 10−16, OR = 1.62, 95% CI: 1.46–1.80; rs9271366, Pgcadj=6.46 × 10−12, OR = 2.06, 95% CI: 1.71–2.50). Other known SLE loci associated were ITGAM, STAT4, TNIP1, NCF2 and IRAK1. We identified a novel locus on 10q24.33 (rs4917385, Pgcadj =1.4×10−8) with a eQTL effect (Peqtl=8.0 × 10−37 at USMG5/miR1307), and describe novel loci. We corroborate SLE-risk loci previously identified in European and Asians. Local ancestry estimation showed that HLA allele risk contribution is of European ancestral origin. Imputation of HLA alleles suggested that autochthonous Native American haplotypes provide protection.

CONCLUSIONS

Our results show the insight gained by studying admixed populations to delineate the genetic architecture that underlies autoimmune and complex diseases.

Introduction

“Hispanics” or “Mestizos” in the Americas are a heterogeneous collection of populations with a complex history of Native American, European, African or Asian admixture, resulting from varying geographic origins and individual lineages1; 2. The influence of genetic factors is important to better understand these populations and provide an opportunity to leverage the ancestral contributions to identify genetic factors that predispose to complex genetic traits.

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) (OMIM # 152700) is the prototypical systemic autoimmune disease with a complex genetic influence. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) in European and European-American populations have identified over 40 genetic loci3; 4. Hispanics are more likely to develop severe SLE, showing an earlier age at onset and severe renal disease5; 6. In fact, global genetic proportions of Native American ancestry, is associated with increased, and European ancestry is associated with decreased risk of developing SLE with severe renal disease6; 7. Consistent with this observation, we have shown that the number of risk alleles for SLE increases with increased global genetic Native American ancestry8. These patterns suggest that ancestry contributes to genetic susceptibility through an increased genetic load of risk alleles, and/or that genetic background of Native American ancestry interacts with European risk loci in disease development8.

With the aim of identifying new genetic risk loci for SLE contributed by the Native heritage of the Americas we report the first large-scale GWAS of individuals from four countries of Latin America and the Unites States diagnosed with SLE. We compared their allele frequencies across the HumanOmni1-Quad v1.0 BeadArray with healthy controls from the same geographical areas. To improve statistical power, we used a genotyped set of out-of-study controls from Mexico. We imputed classic HLA four-digit alleles and analyzed their impact on risk using univariate and stepwise regression. Based on local ancestry estimates, we attempted to identify the ethnic origins of the risk loci. Finally, we examined the risk loci to determine whether any of these are expression quantitative trait loci (eQTL) from publicly available databases. Together the known and novel loci identified in this GWAS provide a unique picture of the genetic architecture of SLE contributed by the Native American ancestry in its admixture with Europeans and define the HLA as a risk factor contributed by the European admixture.

Methods

Samples

All SLE patients were recruited from specialist rheumatology clinics and fulfilled the 1982 American College of Rheumatology criteria9. We used two strategies to enrich samples for Native American ancestry. First, previously genotyped Hispanic, Latin American and US Native American individuals with quality control and a set of 253 ancestry informative markers (AIMs) that were part of the Lupus Large Association 2 Study (LLAS2) were included6; 8; 10. Overall, only thirty five percent of these samples had primarily European and Native American admixture, while the great majority also had Asian, African and <20% Native American ancestry, showing that the Hispanic U.S. and Mestizo Latin American populations are highly admixed. We selected samples with <10% global African or Asian ancestries and at least 20% Native American, as estimated by STRUCTURE and using as reference HapMap data and an in-house reference set of 40 individuals of Nahua origin from Mexico8; 11. Second, we recruited patients from countries historically and demographically known to have a lower frequency of African or Asian ancestries12.

Genotyping and laboratory quality control

We genotyped all samples on the HumanOmni1-Quad v1.0 BeadChip, using manifest H, at the Oklahoma Medical Research Foundation (OMRF) genotyping core facility. We used the Illumina clustering algorithm with GenomeStudio v2011.1, and the Illumina provided scatter profile. Samples and SNPs were required to pass two-step quality control (QC) measurements. Samples with a SNP call rate below 90% were removed from the study. SNPs with a call rate below 90% or Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE) p-value <0.0001 were re-clustered using Genome Studio automatic clustering. The cluster profile of SNPs generating a case-control chi square p-value <0.0001 were manually inspected and adjusted to increase accuracy of genotype calls. Genotyping module v1.9.4 was used to complete the genotype clustering and calling. Cluster plots were examined and samples were excluded if they had a SNP call rate <0.90. In addition we integrated a set of 1,432 out-of-study controls genotyped on the Illumina HumanOmni2.5-Quad from the SIGMA type 2 diabetes study in Mexicans13. Analyses were completed with data aligned to the positive strand.

In addition, 125 samples were genotyped for classical HLA A, B, C, DRB1, DQA1, DQB1 alleles using the LUMINEX system and data used to evaluate the performance of the imputation of classical HLA alleles (see below).

Statistical quality control

We considered SNPs to be of high quality if they had call rates >95%, no evidence of differential missingness between cases and controls (p<0.05), and no evidence of departure from Hardy-Weinberg Expectation proportions (controls p<0.01, cases p<0.000001). We made primary inference based on those SNPs with minor allele frequency (MAF) greater than 1%. Based on SNPs passing the quality control thresholds, we removed samples if there was inconsistency between recorded and genetically inferred gender or excess autosomal heterozygosity. We removed duplicates and first- or second-degree relatives based on identity-by-descent statistics computed by the program KING14. We performed a principal component (PC) analysis using Eigensoft v3.0, after merging with HapMap phase 3 individuals (CEU, YRI, CHB and MEX) as reference. The PC analysis was performed on a subset of SNPs with MAF ≥ 0.05 outside of the known SLE loci and flanking linked regions, after LD-prunning (r2≥0.2). Admixture estimates were computed using ADMIXTURE15 and genetic outliers removed based on the admixture estimates and first five PCs.

Statistical analysis

To test for association between a SNP and case/control status, a logistic regression analysis was computed including the first two PCs as covariates; the two PCs reduced the inflation factor sufficiently so that adding additional PCs as covariates did not further reduced it. The primary analysis reported is the joint analysis of North and South America samples. We confirmed the results were consistent with results from the meta-analysis of North and South American samples computed separately, weighting by sample size and using the program METAL16. Similarly, we found comparable results using GEMMA17. No heterogeneity was observed between North and South American data (see results), supporting the use of the joint analysis. The primary inference was based on the additive genetic model, unless there was significant lack-of-fit to the additive model (p-value<0.05). If there was evidence of departure from an additive model, then inference was based on the most significant result among the dominant, additive and recessive genetic models. The additive and recessive models were computed only if there were at least 10 and 30 individuals homozygous for the minor allele, respectively. For analysis of the X chromosome, the data analysis was first stratified by gender and then meta-analyzed across gender. The results of the GWAS reported are adjusted for the genomic control inflation factor (λGC). These statistical analyses were performed using SNPTEST and SNPGWA version 4.0 as described below. We visually inspected the cluster plots for the most associated SNPs in the regions to confirm the genotyping quality. To determine how many independent associations were within a genomic region, a manual stepwise conditional logistic regression procedure (i.e., forward selection with backward elimination, entry and exit criteria of p-value<0.0001) was computed. Specifically, for each region that reached genome-wide significance, the top SNP was included as a covariate and the association statistics were re-calculated. SNPs were allowed to enter and exit models in this stepwise fashion until no additional SNPs met a significance threshold of p-value <0.0001. To adjust for the number of tests actually computed, we calculated the false discovery rate (FDR) adjusted p-values using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure on the genomic control adjusted p values (Pgcadj). We denote these FDR-adjusted p-values as PFDR18

The cumulative variance explained by common SNPs was estimated using a variance component model and restricted maximum likelihood estimation as implemented in the program GCTA and adjusting for the PCs as covariates using Yang’s correction factor (c=0 from formula 9) for imperfect LD with causal variants. Estimates are based on SNPs that had <1% missing genotypes and stringent relatedness threshold of 0.025.

We constructed regional plots of association for regions of interest using the program Locuszoom.

Imputation

We computed SNP genotype imputation across the genome using the 1000Genomes reference panel to localize the top statistical associations. Specifically, we used the program SHAPEIT to pre-phase separately the Illumina HumanOmni1-Quad genotype data and HumanOmni2.5M genotype data for SNPs that passed quality control. We used IMPUTE2 with the 1000 Genomes Phase 1 integrated reference panel to impute the SNP genotypes for the HumanOmni2.5M. We then used IMPUTE219; 20 to impute the SNP genotypes for the HumanOmni1-Quad with the 1000 Genomes Phase 1 integrated reference panel and the previously imputed HumanOmni2.5M chip. We computed tests of association, ORs, 95% CI’s and other summary statistics on those SNPs that had information scores (> 0.5) and confidence scores (> 0.9). We tested for association between imputed SNPs and SLE status using IMPUTE2’s companion program SNPTEST; SNPTEST explicitly accounts for estimated imputation uncertainty (i.e., the posterior probability) in the test of association.

Classical human leukocyte antigen (HLA) allele imputation

We imputed the classical human leukocyte antigen (HLA) alleles at HLA A, B, C, DPB1, DQA1, DQB1 and DRB1 using the program HiBAG21 and its corresponding Hispanic reference dataset. HiBAG uses an ensemble classifier and bagging techniques to arrive at an average posterior probability for the four-digit classical HLA alleles. Because 37.8% of the reference SNPs used by HiBAG were missing genotype data, we repeated the HLA imputation after filling-in missing genotype data with the “best guess” imputed SNP data from the 1000 genomes imputation described above. By using the “best guess” genotype data that had a posterior probability >0.90, the percent of missing SNPs in the reference set was reduced to 0.6% (see Table S1 for details). However, and as should be expected, we observed that the posterior probabilities for the HLA alleles were comparable for the two methods and we are reporting the HLA imputation based on the imputed data incorporating the imputation posterior probability.

Estimation of Local Ancestry

Local ancestry assignment was performed using PCAdmix22 at K=3 ancestral groups. This approach relies on phased data from reference panels and the admixed individuals. To construct our reference panel we combined publicly available data from 26 West African (YRI), 26 European-descent (CEU) HapMap3 trios23; 24 and 40 of the Nahua individuals. After merging with all Latino individuals and pruning for missing values at 5% and highly linked markers with r2>0.8, the resulting data set comprised 417,563 overlapping SNPs. Individual genotypes from admixed and continental reference samples were phased using BEAGLE 3.0 software 25. An assessment of the accuracy of this approach is given in26.

For each local ancestry window we performed a Welch’s two sample t-test between cases and controls to assess if there was significant contribution of any given ancestral group to each window’s disease association.

Bioinformatic Analysis of eQTL Locus

GENEVAR was used to analyze gene expression variation of the novel locus rs7911488 on UMSG5. Data from HapMap lymphoblasts from individuals of several ancestries was incorporated in the analysis. In addition, ENCODE v3 data was used to investigate transcription factor binding information for the locus. Transfac™ and Match™ interface was used to determine if SNP alleles modified binding of the transcription factors to the target DNA sequence. Only high quality vertebrate matrixes were used, with cut-offs minimizing the sum of the false positive and negatives. Prediction of the RNA folding for each of the alleles was done using the RNAfold. Finally, biogps was used to inform about the cellular expression of USMG5.

Results

The present GWAS contained 3,710 genotyped individuals (1393 SLE cases, 886 controls) and 1431 out-of-study control genotype data from the SIGMA study27. The 1393 SLE cases were composed of 862 individuals of enriched Native American ancestry from Mexico, Hispanics and Native Americans from the United States, collectively denoted North American (NA); and, 531 individuals of enriched Native American ancestry from Argentina, Chile and Peru, collectively denoted as South American (SA). Parallel numbers for within-study-controls were 542 and 344, respectively; all out-of-study controls were of NA origin. The OMNI1 and OMNI2.5 had 996,672 and 1,469,575 SNPs that passed quality control filters, respectively. A total of 580,483 SNPs on both chips are the foundation of the analyses reported here. After quality control, 88.7% of the NA SLE cases and 78.8% NA controls were female; including the NA out-of-study controls reduce that percentage to 65.9% female. Similarly, 91.7% and 89.5% of the SA SLE cases and controls, respectively, were female.

Principal components (PC) analysis identified two PCs that significantly reduced the inflation of our test statistic to 1.18 and were supported by the PC scree plot. The addition of more PCs did not meaningfully impact the inflation. The PC plot shows that two PCs differentiated the populations diverging with a vertex at the European ancestry cluster and NA samples at the top and SA below (Figure S1). These corresponded to a South American population group (N = 875), and a North American group (N = 2,835). The association analysis probability-probability plot is provided as Figure S2. Thus, the analyses reported adjust for two PCs and then use a genomic control adjustment. The joint analysis of NA and SA versus the meta-analysis of NA and SA was carefully examined. Clearly, and because of the out-of-study controls were of NA origin, the statistical power was higher for the NA analysis. As expected, the results were comparable with the joint analysis allowing for a more robust sample size to consider additive and recessive genetic models (Figure S3; Table S1). There were no SNPs with evidence for differential effects between NA and SA that met genome-wide significance or FDR thresholds of significance.

Non-HLA Associations

Results are partitioned into three tiers: Tier 1 P<5×10−8, Tier 2, 5×10−8<P<1×10−6, and Tier 3, not P>1×10−6 and PFDR<0.05. All associations in these tiers meet significance adjusting for the number of tests computed via the Benjamini-Hochberg FDR method. All associations reported have linkage disequilibrium support for the top associations (Table 1). All loci were subjected to stepwise analysis (conditional logistic regression analysis) following imputation. When the result of this analysis surpassed that of the GC adjusted p value, it is shown in the last column of Table 1, and the subscript i, shown by the SNP rs number, when the SNP has been imputed. In total, 7 regions met Tier 1 criteria, 4 regions met Tier 2 criteria and 31 additional regions met Tier 3 criteria (Table 1, Table S2, and Supplemental Materials LocusZoom plots Figure S4).

Multiple previously reported non-HLA SLE-predisposing loci in NA-enriched Hispanic samples are supported in these samples (Table 1). Among the most prominent are ITGAM (rs34572943, Pgcadj = 6.52×10−19; OR = 2.25, 95% CI: 1.91–2.66), STAT4 (rs11889341, Pgcadj = 3.02×10−13; OR = 1.5, 95% CI: 1.36–1.66), TNIP1 (rs7708392, Pgcadj = 2.23×10−11; OR = 1.76, 95% CI: 1.50–2.07), NCF2 (rs13306575, Pgcadj = 3.21×10−8; OR = 1.62, 95% CI: 1.39–1.91), and IRAK1 (rs1059702, Pgcadj = 8.2×10−14; OR = 0.58, 95% CI: 0.49–0.68) in the X chromosome (Table 1). A region previously associated in Europeans that was associated, for the first time in individuals with enriched Native American ancestry in our study at tier 3 level was JAZF1 (Table 1, Figure S4 and Table S2). A region previously associated in Asians and associated with good support for the first time in a second population was WDFY4 at tier level 3, as well as RABGAP1L previously associated as a deletion variant in Koreans28.

Our GWAS identified a number of novel loci associated with SLE. The region on 10q24.33 (rs4917385, Pgcadj = 1.39 × 10−8; OR=0.72, 95% CI: 0.65–0.80; and rs7911488, Pgcadj = 2.48 × 10−7, OR = 0.74, 95% CI: 0.66–0.82) had 27 Tier 1 and 2 SNPs (i.e., p-values <10−6). The association in this region covers several genes (INA, CALHM1, USMG5, PDCD11, PCGF6, NT5C2, COL17A1, CNNM2 and miR1307) (Figure 2). Using stepwise analysis following imputation, the strongest hit explaining the entire association was rs4917385 (P = 9.98 × 10−10) located on RPEL1. Strong LD was observed across the region, and further fine mapping will be needed. Considering the ancestral proximity between Native American and Asian populations, we used data from an ongoing SLE GWAS done on Korean individuals (Lessard, et al., submitted). The start and stop positions on the meta-analysis were based on SNPs that were +/−500kb from rs7911488, with the actual coordinates (b37): chr10:104,661,484 -105,643,134 bp. Importantly, albeit weakly, exactly the same genotyped SNPs showed association in this population (rs11191642, Pdom= 1,02 × 10−2, and rs7911488, Pdom= 1,33 × 10−2, Figure 2, mid panel) as in the Amerindian enriched individuals (rs11191642, Pdom= 3.97 × 10−6, and rs7911488, Pdom= 2,80 × 10−6, Figure 2, top panel) increasing the significance slightly for this locus (rs11191642, Pdom= 8.66 × 10−7, and rs7911488, Pdom= 1,01 × 10−6 Figure 2, bottom panel; Table S3).

eQTL Analysis of the major novel locus

The chromosome 10q24.33 region houses USMG5 and miR1307, where the associated SNP rs7911488 is located in the loop of the hairpin structure of the pre-miRNA of miR130729. SNP rs7911488 shows a cis eQTL effect (Figure S5) for all populations available29; 30. Particularly for the populations of interest, values were, for CEU and MEX, rs7911488, Peqtl = 8.0 × 10−37 and Peqtl = 5.1 × 10−15, respectively, using HapMap lymphoblastoid cell lines30. The data indicated significantly lower USMG5 levels in individuals with the AA genotype and higher levels in those with the GG genotype with heterozygotes intermediate between the two. Examination of the ENCODE transcription factor binding data (v3) revealed that rs7911488 lies within the binding region of several transcription factors. Using high quality Transfac™ matrices we selected the two best transcription factor candidates to bind to the sequence of USMG5. The A variant of rs7911488 exhibited binding sites for two transcription factors: COMP1 (matrix sequence cgagtcGATTGgcaacacagacga, with matrix core in capitals), and RFX1 (gagtcgattgGCAACac). The G variant retained only the binding site for COMP1. Binding only to RFX1 was lost when the risk variant (G) of rs7911488 was present.

The second intriguing mechanism of action for rs7911488 involves the microRNA-1307 (miR1307) gene, which overlaps exon 2 of USMG5. While this microRNA is listed as “provisional” in RefSeq, there is evidence that it is expressed29. rs7911488 lies in the middle of this gene, and while it does not alter the sequence of the mature miR-1307 products, it appears to considerably change the structure of the precursor molecule in a way that would likely render it inactive. Similar to USMG5, miR-1307 is encoded from the negative DNA strand. Therefore, the A/G SNP designation, based on the reference sequence, would be the complement, T/C in miR-1307. Based on the predicted folding of RNA from the two alleles, the T allele forms the double-stranded structure of a precursor microRNA molecule (Figure S6, left). This would result in the molecule binding to the Dicer complex and eventual release of the single-stranded microRNAs. The presence of the C allele is predicted to completely alter the secondary structure of the molecule (Figure S6, right), making it unlikely to bind to the Dicer complex required for trimming. These data suggest that only the T allele would result in an active miR-1307, which would affect all of the downstream targets of this molecule.

MHC Region Association

The HLA region showed several SNPs primarily confined to the class II region, with one peak on the DQA2-DQB1 loci (rs9275572, Pgcadj = 1.11 × 10−16, OR = 1.62, 95% CI: 1.46–1.80) and a second peak on DRB1-DQA1 loci (rs9271366, Pgcadj = 6.46×10−12 OR = 2.06, 95% CI: 1.71–2.50). The latter SNP is tag for the DR2 haplotype of the HLA allele DRB1*15:01 in Europeans31. We observed no association with the tag for DR3 in Europeans, rs2187668 (DRB1*03:01), present in the OMNI1 and 2.5 BeadArrays and passing QC, suggesting that the haplotype structure is different to that of Europeans.

Imputed Classic HLA Allele Associations

The imputation of the HLA alleles identified both known and novel four-digit allele associations with SLE. Table 2 provides the HLA allele associations of Pimp<0.0001 or strongest association by locus. The strongest associations were with DQA1*01:02, DQB1*06:02 and DRB1*15:01, known to conform the common European extended haplotype for DR2, and DQA1*05:01, DQB1*02:01 and DRB1*03:01, known to conform, in turn the European extended haplotype for DR332. Several alleles frequently found in Native American populations were also observed. For instance, alleles frequent in the Mexican population, but not exclusive to it, such as DQB1*03:01 and DQB1*03:02 (Table 2 and Table S4), appeared to confer protection. Particularly, DQB1*03:01 was strongly associated, individually (Pimp = 8.4×10−12; OR = 0.60, 95% CI 0.52–0.69). This DQB1 allele is found in known Native American haplotypes associated with the DRB1*14:06, DRB1*16:02 and DRB1*14:02 alleles (Table S4). A similar effect was observed for DQB1*03:02, which showed a weaker protective association (Pimp = 2.2×10−3; OR = 0.82, 95% CI 0.72–0.93). DQB1*03:02 is found in Native American haplotypes when associated with the DRB1*04:07 or DRB1*04:11 alleles. These DRB1 alleles were also weakly associated with a protective effect (Table S3). These DRB1 alleles also showed individually, weaker associations, all protective, suggesting that Native American haplotypes, in aggregate contribute primarily to protection. Two associations were observed with DRB1 alleles that confer protection and that are found in haplotypes of European ancestry, DRB1*11:04 and DRB1*11:01.

Local ancestry analysis of the HLA locus

To define if any genetic association could involve particular continental ancestries, we performed local ancestry estimations using PCAdmix22. The posterior probabilities can be depicted for each phased chromosome (Figure S7). We analyzed each associated locus for SNP windows showing association between cases and controls in any given ancestry. Confirming our imputation data, the HLA region in chromosome 6 (29.3–33.3Mbp) had 4 continuous windows with 863 SNPs with significant differences in the European ancestry between cases and controls (Figure 3). The reminder of the loci had no specific significant differences in any particular ancestry, suggesting that most loci composed of common variants are found across all continental ancestry borders.

Discussion

This is the first GWAS in SLE patients enriched with Native American ancestry. The opportunity to perform a complete GWAS provides an interesting view of the genetic structure of the population. In this study we have several findings: we clearly show a different pattern of genetic association where the genetic association within the IRF5-TNPO3 locus surpasses that of the MHC, the main and major association for autoimmune disease in Europeans and in most populations. Using two approaches, local ancestry estimation and imputation of classical HLA alleles show that risk for lupus within the HLA region is given by the European ancestral alleles involving DRB1*03:01 and DRB1*15:01, while all autochthonous Amerindian ancestral alleles identified were protective32. It is feasible that the degree of diversity of the Native American populations is such that there are several potential, but low frequency risk alleles or novel functional alleles that require much more power to be consistently associated with SLE and remain to be discovered.

Our present results and our previous observations that individuals with higher Native American global ancestry had increased numbers of risk alleles seem to be contradictory. However, the most plausible explanation is that the number of alleles required to attain a level of risk for SLE in the Native American population is higher.

The imputation of HLA alleles using HiBAG in a primarily Native American population may be a controversial issue due to the scant information available and the complexity of the admixture. To ascertain the fidelity of our data, we performed a comparison of imputed and genotyped alleles in 87 individuals. Agreement between two- and four-digit HLA genotyping with that of HIBAG imputation is high (see Supplemental Figure S8) reinforcing our results and allowing for several conclusions.

Interestingly, the classical haplotype in Europeans for DRB1*15:01, class I loci (A*03:01, B*07:02 and C*07:02), was not associated in this population. This finding suggests that the class II associations are independent and would support a major role for class II and not class I alleles in SLE. This is in agreement with other studies that performed trans-ancestral mapping of the HLA region in SLE32.

We identify 23 completely new suggestive loci. Also, as recent admixture can produce extended blocks of linkage disequilibrium, several of these regions need to be analyzed individually. For example, PCNXL3 is located close to RNASEH2C, a gene implicated in the Aicardi-Goutieres interferonopathy33, a monogenic disease resembling SLE in infants, and PRPS2 is located close to TLR7 in the X chromosome. In one study, the genetic association with TLR7 was found exclusively in males, but a second study found the association in males and females34. We do not observe a male tendency in our study (Table S5). We observe the association in females, while, as expected, the frequency in males, is reduced.

One interesting finding is the strong association of the TNPO3-IRF5 locus. This association surpassed even the MHC suggesting major alternative mechanisms towards disease development. We had previously reported this association in Mexicans35. This locus shows a stronger effect than that for published European lineages36. The reason for this is unclear. IRF5 is a transcription factor clearly involved in the type I interferon and pro-inflammatory signaling, and risk alleles for this locus associate with increased expression of IRF5. Most importantly, studies by Niewold, et al have shown that there are specific associations of IRF5-TNPO3 haplotypes with anti-RBP antibodies (the collective name for anti-Sm, anti-Ro and anti-nRNP antibodies). Both, the IRF5 haplotypes and the serology strongly associated with increased levels of IFNα in serum37, a key feature of SLE. The role of TNPO3, if any, is not understood, but a recent article by Kottyan, et al where both genes were carefully analyzed in several ethnicities showed an independent haplotype that included both genes tagged by SNP rs12534421 that originated in European or European-admixed populations but not found in Africans from HapMap or 1000 genomes. Another haplotype was found in all ethnicities tagged by SNP rs4728142 and located in the promoter of IRF5. Local admixture estimations done showed increased European admixture at the IRF5-TNPO3 locus in African-American cases38. Our local ancestry analysis in our admixed Amerindian-European sample does not specifically provide evidence for increased European ancestry within the IRF5-TNPO3 locus although this possibility appears much likely and would further support a role of European ancestry in the inheritance of IRF5 SNPs in disease susceptibility, as we previously proposed35.

Local ancestry analysis provided information on the impact of other ancestries in our loci, and interestingly, none with the exception of the HLA showed a clear ancestral “bias”. This could require a larger sample size. Fine structural consequences of admixture and their impact on the development of disease in rapidly expanding and admixed populations is important to understand so we can eventually define differences that might have an impact on the mechanisms of disease development and the design of new individual therapies.

Our data suggesting the involvement of the USMG5 gene and the miR-1307 requires further investigation. Whether the expression of this microRNA may impact numerous targets of importance in lupus needs to be experimentally tested. USMG5 is widely expressed in the central nervous system, but also in CD105+ endothelial cells, CD34+ hematopoietic stem cells, dendritic, myeloid and NK cells. RFX1, the transcription factor whose binding was abolished according to Transfac analysis, is known to regulate expression of the HLA class II molecules. RFX1 was recently found to regulate the expression of CD11a and CD70 in T cells from SLE patients39.

In conclusion, we suggest the existence of 23 novel loci for lupus in this population that need replication and one completely novel locus in chromosome 10 replicated in Asians. Several of the major genes for lupus previously confirmed in Europeans and Asians are also associated in this population showing that the major genes for this disease are clearly established and associated across ethnicities.
