Summary

L-arginine is the essential precursor of nitric oxide, and is involved in multiple key physiological processes, including vascular and immune function. The genetic regulation of blood L-arginine levels is largely unknown. We performed a genome-wide association study (GWAS) to identify genetic factors determining serum L-arginine levels, amongst 901 Europeans and 1,394 Indian Asians. We show that common genetic variations at the KLKB1 and F12 loci are strongly associated with serum L-arginine levels. The G allele of SNP rs71640036 (T/G) in KLKB1 is associated with lower serum L-arginine concentrations (10 μmol/L per allele copy, p=1×10-24), while allele T of rs2545801 (T/C) near the F12 gene is associated with lower serum L-arginine levels (7 μmol/L per allele copy, p=7×10-12). Together these two loci explain 7% of the total variance in serum L-arginine concentrations. The associations at both loci were replicated in independent cohorts with plasma L-arginine measurements (p<0.004). The two sentinel SNPs are in nearly complete LD with the nonsynonymous SNP rs3733402 at KLKB1 and the 5’-UTR SNP rs1801020 at F12, respectively. SNPs at both loci are associated with blood pressure. Our findings provide new insight into the genetic regulation of L-arginine and its potential relationship with cardiovascular risk.

Keywords: Serum L-arginine concentration, genome-wide association, coagulation, kallikrein-kinin system

Introduction

L-arginine is a naturally occurring semi-essential amino acid, which plays a key biological role in cardiovascular physiology, immune function and hormonal signalling. L-arginine is the substrate for the synthesis of the potent vasodilator nitric oxide (NO) by the endothelial NO synthase (eNOS) in blood vessels. NO is crucial in maintaining vascular homeostasis, and its depletion may contribute to the development of hypertension, atherosclerosis, endothelial dysfunction and vascular inflammation (1–5). L-arginine bioavailability is a rate limiting factor in the synthesis of NO (3, 4) and low levels of circulating L-arginine are reported in a number of diseases and conditions (6). Oral administration or infusion of L-arginine has shown the beneficial effect of improved endothelia function and reduced blood pressure (7, 8).

Recent studies suggest that L-arginine concentrations are heritable, and associated with genetic variation in ARG1 (9). In contrast, several studies using metabolome wide profiling, with semi-quantitative measurement of L-arginine, fail to identify genetic variation influencing L-arginine concentrations (10–16). The genetic factors regulating L-arginine levels are not well understood.

We performed a GWAS of serum L-arginine levels using targeted quantification of L-arginine by mass spectrometry (MS), to identify the genetic factors influencing L-arginine and investigate the potential relationships between L-arginine metabolism and cardiovascular traits.

Materials and methods

Samples and phenotypes at the discovery stage

We investigated 2,487 participants selected at random from the London Life Sciences Prospective Population (LOLIPOP) Study (17, 18), comprising 998 Europeans and 1,489 Indian Asians. The LOLIPOP study is approved by the local Research Ethics Committee, and all participants provided written consent for genetic studies.

Participants were interviewed by trained research nurses according to a standardized protocol. An interviewer-administered questionnaire was used to collect data on demographic factors, tobacco and alcohol consumption, as well as personal and family medical history. Indian Asians had all four grandparents born on the Indian subcontinent (India, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, or Bangladesh). Europeans were of self-reported white ancestry. Anthropometric (height, weight, waist and hip circumference) and blood pressure measurements were recorded. Blood samples were collected after an 8 hour fast for measurement of fasting glucose, hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c), lipid profile, creatinine, and high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hsCRP). Aliquots of whole blood and serum were stored at -80°C.

Serum L-arginine measurement at the discovery stage

Serum concentrations of L-arginine were measured by liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) using a Prominence LC-20ADXR binary pump (Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan) coupled to a QTRAP 5500 hybrid triple quadropole mass spectrometer (AB Sciex, Framingham, MA, US). Serum (25 μL) was mixed with equal volumes of distilled water, 50 mM ammonium hydroxide, and a solution containing isotopically labelled internal standard, d7-Arg (Cambridge Isotopes Laboratories, Andover, MA, US). Dithioerythritol (25 μL, 100 mM) was added and the samples were left for 15 min followed by protein precipitation using 25 μL perchloric acid (3.3 M) and centrifugation at 14’000 rpm for 10 min. Supernatant (5 μL) was then added to 195 μL NH4+ (10 mM), and 7 μL was subsequently injected onto a Kinetex PFP column (100 × 2.10 mm, 2.6 μm; Phenomenex, Torrance, CA, US) kept at 35°C. Gradient elution at 300 μL/min was employed with mobile phases A (0.05 % formic acid in water, v/v) and B (0.1 % formic acid in methanol, v/v). 0% B was initially held at for 1.5 min, followed by a linear gradient reaching 10% B at 4.0 min, and 100% B at 4.3 min, and held there for 1.5 min, followed by equilibration at 0% B for 2 min. Gas 1 and 2 were set at 50, collision gas at high, and the curtain gas, ion spray voltage, and source temperature were set at 35, 4000 V, and 700°C, respectively. The following ion transitions were used for quantitation in multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) mode: m/z 175 → m/z 70 for L-arginine, and m/z 182 → m/z 77 for d7-Arg. Optimisation of MRM transitions, data acquisition, and analysis were performed with Analyst 1.6.1 (AB Sciex, Framingham, MA, US). L-arginine was quantified by comparing the area ratio against the internal standard with calibration curves constructed in control plasma. For each batch, a number of randomly chosen samples, as well as all identified outliers, were identified and repeated to ensure accurate quantitation. Inter- and intra-assay coefficients of variation for L-arginine assessed at three QC levels were ~7%, well below the accepted limit recommended by the European Medicines Agency (see URL).

Genotyping and imputation at the discovery stage

DNA was extracted from whole blood. Genome-wide genotyping was carried out using the Illumina Human610 array, as previously described (17), in 901 Europeans and 1,394 Indian Asians from amongst the 2,487 LOLIPOP participants with L-arginine measurements. SNPs with minor allele frequencies <1%, p-values for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (p-HWE) <1x10-6, or missing rates >5% were removed. Exclusion criteria for samples were duplicates, ethnic outliers, cryptic relatedness, gender discrepancy, extreme heterogeneity, missing rate >5%, or incomplete phenotype data.

Imputation of un-genotyped variants was performed in European (544,620 directly genotyped SNPs) and Indian Asian (541,497 SNPs) samples separately with IMPUTE2 v2.3.0 (19), using the cosmopolitan 1000 Genomes haplotypes (phase 1, March 2012 version, genomic build 37) as reference (20). The reference haplotypes most relevant to the study population by allelic consistency are chosen automatically and it has shown that the cosmopolitan reference haplotypes produce high imputation accuracy (20).

Replication studies

The lead SNPs identified at the discovery stage were tested in two independent cohorts with plasma L-arginine measurements. All participants in the replication studies provided written informed consent for genetic studies, and the study protocols were approved by local Institutional review boards and ethical committees.

The European Prospective Investigation into Cancer, Norfolk (EPIC-Norfolk) is a prospective population cohort of men and women of European origin aged 40-79 years living in Norfolk, UK, recruited from general practices between 1993 and 1998 (21). A nested case-control study of incident stroke was performed with an average follow-up of eleven years (N = 1,000). Controls were matched to cases on age and gender. Clinical and lifestyle data and blood samples were collected at baseline when all the cases and controls were stroke free. Baseline plasma L-arginine levels were measured with a modified approach as in our discovery study. Genotyping was performed on the Affymetrix 500K arrays, followed by quality control and imputation to Phase 1 1000 Genomes reference panel.

The Framingham Heart Study (FHS) is a longitudinal observational, community-based cohort initiated in 1948 in Framingham, MA, USA, to investigate risk factors prospectively for cardiovascular diseases (22). In this study, 2,981 participants of the Framingham Offspring Cohort (FOC), who had complete genotypic (Affymetrix 500K and 50K) and plasma L-arginine measurements were analysed after exclusions. The FOC consists of children (and children’s spouses) of the original Framingham cohort participants. Details for plasma L-arginine measurement, genotyping and imputation were described previously (9).

Statistical analysis

In the discovery cohorts, outliers with serum L-arginine levels exceeding 3 standard deviations from the means in males or females separately were removed from further analysis. Continuous and discrete traits were compared between Europeans and Indian Asians using one-way ANOVA and Chi-square tests respectively with R (version 2.15.0, Table 1). Linear regression was used to investigate the associations between serum L-arginine levels and other phenotypic traits, with adjustment for age, gender and ethnicity (Table S1).

Principal components (PCs) of the genotype data were obtained with smartpca in EIGENSOFT using ~100K linkage disequilibrium (LD)-pruned genome-wide SNPs. Linear regression with additive genetic effect for association and conditional analysis were performed with SNPTEST (v2.4.1), adjusted for age, gender, and PCs 1-5 (23). No data transformation was applied to serum L-arginine as it conformed approximately to a normal distribution. Markers with minor allele frequency (MAF) <0.01, p-HWE <1×10-6, or imputation quality score <0.4 were excluded at the discovery stage. A total of 28.7M markers were obtained before quality control, in which about 8M SNPs retained after filtering for further analysis. The association of SNPs with L-arginine concentrations was tested in Europeans and Indian Asians separately, and association test results corrected for the genomic inflation factors. We then carried out inverse variance meta-analyses across the two ethnic groups using a fixed effects model as implemented in METAL (24). Regional plots for the associated loci were generated using LOCUSZOOM using genome build GRCh37/hg19 and 1000 Genome Nov 2014 European (EUR) population for linkage disequilibrium (LD) (25). Trait variance explained by the sentinel (lead) SNP(s) was calculated in Europeans or Indian Asians separately, using linear regression model with covariates adjusted as above. The r2 from the regression model was taken as the estimates of variance explained.

In replication studies, association analyses of the sentinel or proxy SNPs and plasma L-arginine were carried out in FHS, using a linear mixed effects model with relationship coefficient matrix to account for familial correlation, adjusted for sex, age, diabetes mellitus, systolic and diastolic blood pressure, smoking, body mass index, and serum creatinine levels. In EPIC-Norfolk, association analyses were performed in cases (N = 500) and controls (N = 500) separately, under the additive genetic model with adjustment for age and gender. In meta-analyses of combined discovery and replication studies, we used the weighted Z-score method in METAL along with a random-effects model in METASOFT to account for heterogeneity between discovery and replication studies (26).

Expression quantitative trait loci (eQTLs) analysis

We analysed the relationship between the sentinel SNPs influencing L-arginine concentrations and the expression of genes within 1Mb either side of the sentinel SNPs in peripheral blood leucocytes amongst 816 Indian Asian participants of the LOLIPOP study. Bonferroni correction was applied for testing multiple genes. In brief, transcriptome-wide gene expression in peripheral blood leucocytes was measured using the Illumina Human HT-12 v4 expression BeadChip according to manufacturer’s protocol in two LOLIPOP cohorts of Indian Asian participants. Negative control-probes were used to calculate detection p-value threshold (p<10-9, corresponding to p<0.05 adjusted for the number of tests). Transcripts with call-rates < 10% were removed. Expression intensities were read and processed using the R packages Illuminaio and Limma. Gene expression values were subjected to background correction, quantile normalisation and log2 transformation. Linear regression was applied to the processed expression data adjusted for age, gender, RNA integrity number, RNA conversion batch, scanning batch, and array position. Residuals from the regression were obtained for the following genetic association analysis.

Genotyping of the two cohorts was carried out using Illumina OmniExpress (N=612) or Illumina OmniExpressExome (N=204) whole-genome arrays. Standard quality control of GWAS was applied and genotype imputation was performed using the 1000 Genomes cosmopolitan reference panel as described above. Association between the residuals of the gene expression and the sentinel SNPs (rs71640036 vs KLKB1 and rs2545801 vs F12) was carried out with Quicktest separately in the two datasets, adjusted for age, gender, and PCs 1-5. METAL was used to combine the effects of both cohorts by inverse-variance meta-analysis. Association results were corrected for genomic inflation factors before and after meta-analysis.

Finally we examined the association of our sentinel or proxy SNPs with gene expression using published data for liver and other tissues (27–29), using the Genotype-Tissue Expression (GTEx) data (30).

Association with cardiovascular and metabolic traits

We examined the relationship of the sentinel SNPs influencing L-arginine concentrations with blood pressure, cardiovascular disease and metabolic traits using public databases and the NHGRI GWAS Catalog (31). Where the sentinel SNP was not available in the respective dataset, we used the best available proxy SNP, selected based on LD (r2>0.9).

Results

Characteristics of participants

The characteristics of the discovery and replication study samples are described in Tables 1 and S2, respectively. Serum L-arginine levels are higher among Indian Asians than Europeans (p=0.01). Indian Asians also have higher rates of coronary heart disease and type 2 diabetes, and higher measurements of waist-hip ratio and glycemic traits (fasting glucose, fasting insulin, and HbA1c), and lower levels of total and HDL cholesterol, compared to Europeans (p<0.05, Table 1).

Genome-wide association in the discovery study

The LOLIPOP GWAS genomic inflation factors were 1.008 for Europeans, 1.004 for Indian Asians, and 1.002 for meta-analysis in the discovery study. Quantile-quantile (QQ) plots show evidence for association between genetic variation and serum L-arginine levels in both ethnic groups and in combined analysis (Figure S1). Twenty-four SNPs in the European samples and fifty-nine SNPs in the Indian Asian samples reach genome-wide significance for association with serum L-arginine levels (p<5×10-8, Figure S2), all on chromosome 4. In combined analysis, 82 SNPs are associated with L-arginine levels, comprising 77 SNPs at the KLKB1 locus (kallikrein B, plasma (Fletcher Factor) 1) on chromosome 4, and 5 SNPs at the F12 locus (Coagulation Factor XII (Hageman Factor)) on chromosome 5 (Table S3, Figure S2).

KLKB1 locus

The SNP with the lowest p-value (rs71640036, p=9.7×10-25, the sentinel SNP) is an intron variant in the KLKB1 gene (Tables 2 and S3). The G allele of rs71640036 is associated with decreased serum L-arginine levels at a rate 10 μmol/L per allele copy (Table 3). The sentinel SNP is highly significantly associated with serum L-arginine levels in both ethnic groups and there is no significant heterogeneity between the two groups (Tables 2 and S3).

A missense non-synonymous SNP rs3733402 (Asn143Ser), which is 3,086 base pairs upstream of the sentinel SNP is also highly significantly associated with serum L-arginine levels (p=1.0×10-23, Table S3). SNP rs3733402 is in high LD with the sentinel SNP (r2 = 0.964 in Europeans and 0.981 in Indian Asians). The G allele of rs3733402 is associated with decreased serum L-arginine levels. Conditional analyses on either the sentinel SNP rs71640036 or rs3733402 showed that none of the other SNPs are significantly associated with serum L-arginine levels (p>0.05 after Bonferroni correction) in this region (±500 kb from the sentinel SNP, covering the KLKB1, CYP4V2, F11 and other nearby genes, data not shown).

F12 locus

The five SNPs significantly associated with serum L-arginine levels on chromosome 5 are in or near the F12 gene. The sentinel SNP rs2545801 (p=6.8×10-12) is intergenic, but is in high LD with rs1801020 (r2 = 0.993 in Europeans and 0.996 in Indian Asians) in the highly conserved 5’ untranslated (promoter) region of F12 gene, itself strongly associated with L-arginine levels (p=1.2×10-11, Table S3). The minor allele T of the sentinel SNP is associated with decreased serum L-arginine levels (7 μmol/L per allele copy, Table 3). There is no significant heterogeneity between the two ethnic groups. Conditional analyses on the sentinel SNP rs2545801 showed that there are no secondary associations with serum L-arginine levels in this region (±500 kb from the sentinel SNP, data not shown). Together the sentinel SNPs from both KLKB1 and F12 loci explain approximately 7% of the total variance of serum L-arginine after adjusting for age, gender, and PCs 1-5 (Table 2).

Replication studies

Combined analyses of the replication cohorts showed significant associations at both the KLKB1 and F12 loci (p<0.004, Table 2). Meta-analysis combining discovery and replication studies also showed strong associations with circulating L-arginine levels (Table 2).

Suggestive associations and other analyses

In combined analysis of the European and Indian Asian samples in the discovery study, 129 SNPs showed p-values <1×10-5 but >5×10-8 with consistent direction of effects in both ethnic groups (Table S4). Two intron SNPs in ARG1 gene and 7 SNPs in the nearby MED23 gene on chromosome 6 are associated with serum L-arginine levels (lowest p=5 ×10-7, Table S4). Other regions with suggestive associations are in non-gene regions of chromosomes 2 and 11, SOX6 (11p15.3), PRH1-PRR4 (12p), GPR133 (12q24.33), HS3ST4 (16p11.2), RP11-638 (18q22.1), and TSHZ2 (20q13.2) (Table S4).

We separately performed association analyses with dominant or recessive models; no additional loci reached genome-wide significance. Gene-gene interaction analysis did not show any synergistic effects for the SNPs between the KLKB1 and F12 genes beyond their additive effect (data not shown).

Gene expression studies

SNP rs2545801 is associated with expression of F12 (p=0.04 after Bonferroni correction), and SNP rs71640036 is associated with expression of CYP4V2 (p=0.01, underlying mechanisms unknown) in our peripheral blood leucocyte samples (Table S5). In contrast there was no significant association of rs71640036 with expression of KLKB1 (p=0.53). The sentinel SNPs were not significantly associated with expression levels of any other genes within the regions (±1Mb). Both KLKB1 and F12 genes are predominantly expressed in liver (30). SNP rs2731672, which is in high LD (r2 = 0.994 in Europeans and 0.987 in Indian Asians) with the sentinel SNP rs2545801 at the F12 locus is strongly associated with F12 gene expression in liver (p=1.2×10-23) (27).

Association of sentinel SNPs with phenotypic traits

Published studies show that the sentinel SNPs at the KLKB1 and F12 loci are also strongly associated with other phenotypic traits including levels of phenylalanine (32, 33), des-arg(9) bradykinin (12, 15), active plasma renin (34), midregional-proadrenomedullin (MR-pro-ADM) and C-terminal-pro-endothelin-1 (35) (Table S6). In addition, the KLKB1 SNPs are associated with histidine (32), leucylphenylalanine (14), HXGXA (14), plasma B-Type Natriuretic Peptide (36) and ratios of serum amino acid levels (32), while F12 SNPs are highly associated with blood FXII antigen levels (37) and prolonged activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) (38) and plasma renin activity (39) (Table S6).

Association of sentinel SNPs with cardiovascular disease

SNP rs71640036 at the KLKB1 locus is associated with both systolic and diastolic blood pressure amongst 134,773 participants of UK Biobank (p=9x10-5 and p=8x10-3 respectively, Table 4). SNP rs2545801 at the F12 locus is also associated with systolic blood pressure (p=8x10-3) but not with diastolic blood pressure. There were no associations of rs71640036 or rs2545801 with coronary heart disease or hsCRP, after Bonferroni correction for multiple testing (Tables 4 and S6).

Discussion

We show that common genetic variation at the KLKB1 and F12 loci is an important determinant of serum L-arginine concentrations. KLKB1 and F12 both encode components of the kinin system. KLKB1 (4q35.2) encodes prekallikrein, which is a precursor of the serine protease plasma kallikrein. F12 (5q35.3) encodes the coagulation Factor XII (FXII), a serine protease involved in coagulation activation through the intrinsic pathway. Kallikrein and FXII are closely linked biologically and activate each other in a reciprocal fashion.

We found that nonsynonymous SNP rs3733402 in the KLKB1 gene, which is in high LD with the sentinel SNP rs71640036, is associated with decreased serum L-arginine levels. This SNP leads to the replacement of asparagine with serine at codon 143 in exome 5 of KLKB1 gene, located in apple domain A2 of plasma kallikrein. Previous studies show that this non-synonymous SNP reduces the binding activity of apple domain A2 to circulating high molecular weight kininogen (HMWK), and is associated with low levels of plasma prekallikrein and bradykinin metabolites (40).

The minor A allele of SNP rs1801020 in the promoter region of the F12 gene, which was nearly in complete LD with the sentinel SNP rs2545801 and rs2731672, is associated with reduced serum L-arginine levels in our study, decreased plasma FXII concentrations (37, 41, 42) and prolonged aPTT (38). The SNP is located 4 bases upstream of the translation initiation codon of factor XII. Experiments using reporter constructs suggest that the altered upstream open reading frame (uORF) through the minor T allele is responsible for >50% reduction of FXII protein levels and <30% reduction of mRNA levels (43). The eQTL data showed that these genetic variants are associated with reduced F12 gene expression in liver and blood leucocytes.

The mechanisms linking genetic variants in KLKB1 and F12 to serum L-arginine concentrations remain to be determined. We note that both KLKB1 and F12 are involved in bradykinin release from HMWK. Bradykinin is a nonapeptide with the amino acid sequence Arg-Pro-Pro-Gly-Phe-Ser-Pro-Phe-Arg and is rapidly degraded once it has been generated in cardiovascular system. Aminopeptidase P (APP) cleaves positions 1-2, while carboxypeptidase N (CPN) cleaves positions 8-9, both enzymatic reactions lead to the release of L-arginine (44–46). These observations raise the possibility that genetic variation at KLKB1 and F12 may influence blood L-arginine levels through an effect on bradykinin release and degradation.

Although the role of nitric oxide in human physiology is well described, the relationship of circulating L-arginine metabolism with human disease is uncertain. We found that SNP rs71640036 in the KLKB1 gene, which is strongly associated with lower L-arginine levels, is also associated with lower blood pressure and the F12 variant is associated with higher systolic blood pressure. These findings raise the possibility that L-arginine may be involved in blood pressure regulation. However, since plasma kallikrein and FXII participate in multiple key signalling pathways (35, 37, 47), the association between their genetic variants and blood pressure through L-arginine needs further investigation.

There is increasing evidence showing associations between variants in the KLKB1 and F12 loci and cardiovascular biomarkers (35, 36). The sentinel SNPs, however, showed only borderline or non-significant associations with CAD in the recent large-scale CARDIoGRAMplusC4D study (p=0.02 for the KLKB1 variant) (48). This reflects the complex and multifactorial aetiology of CAD and these loci may contribute to the risk of CAD through intermediate traits, such as blood pressure and thrombosis. The same applies to hsCRP, a marker of systemic inflammation. Given that hsCRP is highly associated with serum L-arginine concentrations in our data and the KLKB1/F12 genes are involved in inflammation, further studies are required to explore the relation between L-arginine and hsCRP.

The kinin system also plays an important role in the contact activation of the intrinsic pathway in coagulation and fibrinolysis. A recent study indicated that the intrinsic pathway-dependent thrombin generation may be a risk factor for ischemic stroke and the F12 variant is associated with thrombin generation (49). Factor XI (FXI), encoded by the F11 gene, is a key factor in the intrinsic pathway and consistently associated with thrombosis risk. The KLKB1 and F11 genes are closely located but with moderate LD (r2 up to 0.3). Our study revealed no association of F11 variants with serum L-arginine levels after exclusion of the effects from the KLKB1 locus. How the L-arginine metabolism affects thrombosis risk remains unclear.

In conclusion, we have identified genetic variation in the KLKB1 and F12 genes as important determinants of serum levels of L-arginine, and provide potential evidence for their role in blood pressure regulation. Our findings provide new insight into the pathways influencing metabolism of L-arginine, the essential substrate in the synthesis of NO, and may help resolve the understanding of the pathways linking L-arginine with endothelial function and cardiovascular health.
