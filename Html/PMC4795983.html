Abstract

Objectives

Schizophrenia is a highly disabling psychiatric disorder with a heterogeneous phenotypic appearance. We aimed to further the understanding of some of the underlying genetics of schizophrenia, using left superior temporal gyrus (STG) gray matter thickness reduction as an endophenoptype in a genome-wide association (GWA) study.

Methods

Structural magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and genetic data of the Mind Clinical Imaging Consortium (MCIC) study of schizophrenia were used to analyze the interaction effects between 1,067,955 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) and disease status on left STG thickness in 126 healthy controls and 113 patients with schizophrenia. We next used a pathway approach to detect underlying pathophysiological pathways that may be related to schizophrenia.

Results

No SNP by diagnosis interaction effect reached genome-wide significance (5 × 10-8) in our GWA study, but ten SNPs reached p-values less than 10-6. The most prominent pathways included those involved in insulin, calcium, PI3K-Akt and MAPK signaling.

Conclusions

Our strongest findings in the GWA study and pathway analysis point towards an involvement of glucose metabolism in left STG thickness reduction in patients with schizophrenia only. These results are in line with recently published studies, which showed an increased prevalence of psychosis among patients with metabolic syndrome-related illnesses including diabetes.

Keywords: Genetics, Imaging, Schizophrenia, Superior Temporal Gyrus, Cortical Thickness

Introduction

Schizophrenia has a lifetime risk of around 1% (World Health Organization, 2008) and is one of the most disabling psychiatric disorders in adults. The genetic and non-genetic architecture of schizophrenia is complex and consequently different approaches including candidate gene studies, GWA studies and gene-environment studies are needed to reveal the pathogenesis of this psychiatric disorder. Although there has been many decades of research on schizophrenia during the modern era of genetics technology (Rees et al., 2015), we are still far from fully understanding the complex phenotype of schizophrenia and its genetic background. The majority of genes which have been found to show significant associations with schizophrenia within recent years could not explain much of the variance associated with schizophrenia (Purcell et al., 2014). Genetic and phenotypic heterogeneity, together with low reliability and long-term stability of clinical diagnoses, could be possible reasons for this. To overcome some of these problems, researchers have focused on GWA studies using endophenotypes (Meyer-Lindenberg and Weinberger, 2006). Endophenotypes, also known as intermediate phenotypes, which by definition are heritable and disease-associated, have the advantage of being reliably measurable, stable and continuously distributed (so called “quantitative traits”), and are thought to be closer to the underlying genetics than the illness category (e.g., schizophrenia) (Gottesman and Gould, 2003).

A variety of endophenotypes has been established in psychiatric research. They can be for example neurophysiological, biochemical or neuropsychological in nature. Potential endophenotypes of schizophrenia have also emerged from neuroimaging, e.g., structural imaging studies. Two examples for neuroimaging endophenotypes are the volume and the thickness of the superior temporal gyrus (STG) (Pearlson, 1997; Rimol et al., 2010). The STG includes Heschl's gyrus as well as the planum temporale and is involved in auditory processes, speech, language, communication and social cognition (Pelphrey et al., 2004). Both an abnormal decrease of STG gray matter volume and cortical thinning in the STG, particularly in the left hemisphere (McCarley et al., 1993; Hirayasu et al., 2000; Nesvåg et al., 2008), can be seen in patients with schizophrenia as well as in first-degree relatives of patients with schizophrenia (Kasai et al., 2003; Yoshida et al., 2009; Tang et al., 2012). Reduced STG volume is also associated with auditory hallucinations (Barta et al., 1990; Flaum et al., 1995), a core positive symptom of schizophrenia. Also, Tang et al. found a negative correlation between gray matter volume in left middle and superior temporal gyrus and PANSS-positive symptoms (Tang et al., 2012). Interestingly, psychosis-like symptoms can be observed during electrical stimulation of the STG (Penfield and Perot, 1963) and neurological conditions such as epilepsy or stroke affecting the STG can lead to schizophrenia-like disturbances of thought, hallucinations and delusions (Galasko et al., 1988).

To investigate the genetic background of STG alterations in patients with schizophrenia, we focused on left STG gray matter thickness, because (a) cortical thickness measures have been reported to have advantages over volume measures for gene discovery approaches (Winkler et al., 2010), (b) heritability estimates of STG thickness (h2 = 0.79) is higher than for STG gray matter volume (measurement in the volume-based representation; h2 = 0.68) (Winkler et al., 2010), and (c) the aforementioned relationship between reduced left STG thickness and hallucinations was also present in our sample (n = 218, r = -0.177, p = 0.009; SM Figure 1.1). Moreover, our data showed a highly significant difference between patients and controls in left STG thickness.

So far, many studies have focused on genetic main effects. For example, international multi-site consortia including more (healthy) participants were recently able to detect genome-wide significant variants influencing the volume of subcortical brain structures and the intracranial volume (Hibar et al., 2015). Here, we examined, however, SNP by diagnosis interaction terms in a GWA study in order to focus on effects potentially specific to and related to schizophrenia. To identify underlying biological processes of the associated genes, we also pursued a pathway approach. To our knowledge, this study was the first to use STG thickness as an endophenotype in a SNP by diagnosis interaction GWA analysis.

Methods and Materials

Participants

The Mind Clinical Imaging Consortium (MCIC) study of schizophrenia (Gollub et al., 2013) was established to further the understanding of the underlying pathophysiological mechanisms of schizophrenia through structural magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and a large number of clinical and neurophysiological measures. Imaging and behavior data from the MCIC study are available via the collaborative informatics and neuroimaging suit (COINS) data exchange (http://coins.mrn.org/dx; Wood et al., 2014). Within this study, 184 healthy controls and 185 patients with chronic or first-episode schizophrenia matched for gender, age, ethnicity, handedness and parental socio-economic status were recruited and scanned at one of the four participating sites: Massachusetts General Hospital in Boston (MGH) and the Universities of Iowa (UI), Minnesota (UMN) and New Mexico (UNM). As required by the Institutional Review Board of record all participants informed their consent in a written way. The patient group (SCZ) relevant for our study design consists of subjects with a DSM-IV diagnosis of schizophrenia (n = 106), schizoaffective (n = 3) or schizophreniform (n = 4) disorder, established using structured clinical interviews and review of case files by trained clinicians. Healthy controls (HC) (n = 126) were included if they had no history of a medical or Axis I psychiatric diagnosis. All participants were required to be at least 18 years of age and not older than 60 and to be fluent in English. Participants were excluded if they had a history of neurologic disease, or psychiatric disease other than schizophrenia, history of a head injury with loss of consciousness, history of substance abuse or dependence within the past month, severe or disabling medical conditions, contraindication to MR scanning or IQ less than 70 (based on the reading subtest from the WRAT3).

Clinical Measures

Prior to subject enrolment, clinicians from all four MCIC sites participated in a two-day training session, during which cross-site inter-rater reliability for the primary diagnostic and symptom-rating scales was established (>85% concordance with videotaped training material). All study participants underwent an extensive clinical diagnostic assessment that included either the SCID-I/P or NP (First, 2002) or the Comprehensive Assessment of Symptoms and History (CASH) (Andreasen et al., 1992). The Wide Range-Achievement Test (WRAT3-RT) (Wilkison, 1993) was used to get valid and reliable information on premorbid cognitive achievement. Parental socio-economic status (SES) and handedness were recorded using the Hollingshead index (Hollingshead, 1965) and Annett Scale of Hand Preference (Annett, 1970), respectively. For patients, detailed information of clinical variables were gathered including length of illness and severity of positive and negative symptoms using the Scale for the Assessment of Positive Symptoms (SAPS) (Andreasen, 1984)/ Scale for the Assessment of Negative Symptoms (SANS) (Andreasen, 1983). Moreover, information of antipsychotic history was collected as part of the psychiatric assessment using the PSYCH instrument (Andreasen, 1987). Cumulative and current antipsychotic exposure was calculated using the chlorpromazine conversion factors (Woods, 2003).

Structural Image Acquisition

A T1-weighted structural MRI with a coronal gradient echo sequence was acquired at each of the four sites using 1.5T Siemens Sonata (MGH, UI, UNM) or a 3T Siemens Trio (UMN). The following parameters were used: TR=2530ms for 3T, TR=12ms for 1.5T; TE=3.79 for 3T, TE=4.76ms for 1.5T; TI=1100 for 3T; Bandwidth=181 for 3T, Bandwidth=110 for 1.5T; voxel size: 0.625×0.625; slice thickness 1.5mm; FOV, 256×256×128 cm matrix; FOV=16cm; NEX=1 for 3T, NEX=3 for 1.5T. According to the guidelines developed by the biomedical informatics research network (BIRN) test bed for morphometry (Jovicich et al., 2006, 2009) a preceding study was implemented with human phantoms to establish cross-site MRI acquisition calibration and reliability.

Structural Image Data Processing

Data of the MCIC structural MRI scans of three consecutive volumes were registered, motion corrected and averaged before they were analyzed in an automated manner with the atlas-based FreeSurfer software suite (http://surfer.nmr.mgh.harvard.edu, Version 4.0.1). Volumetric segmentation, cortical surface reconstruction (Fischl et al., 1999; Fischl and Dale, 2000; Fischl et al., 2002; Desikan et al., 2006) and the estimation of total intracranial volume (Buckner et al., 2004) were part of this analyzing process.

Segmentation and surface reconstruction quality were assured by manual inspection of all raw MRI volumes, segmented volumes in three planes and pial as well as inflated volumes. Five participants' MRI data failed the aforementioned quality assurance but could then be recovered with minor manual intervention following the FreeSurfer user guidelines. STG thickness is a standard output of FreeSurfer.

Genotyping

Blood samples were obtained of each MCIC participant and sent to the Harvard Partners Center for Genetics and Genomics. DNA extraction and genotyping was performed according to the manufacturer's protocol and blinded for group assignment (for more information, see also SM 2.1). Genotyping was performed at the Mind Research Network (MRN) Neurogenetics Core Lab using the Illumina HumanOmni-Quad BeadChip interrogating 1,140,419 SNPs. Normalized bead intensity data obtained for each sample were loaded into GenomeStudio2010 software, which generated SNP genotypes from fluorescent intensities using the manufacturer's default cluster settings. The raw genotypic data were imported into a genome-wide data management system (Laboratory Information Management System) to allow the tracking of individual samples, quality control and the export of user defined formats compatible with the genetic programs used for statistical analysis.

Before quality control (QC), we imputed 56,389 SNPs with the help of IMPUTE2 (Howie et al., 2009).

QC steps included common standard procedures (Teo, 2008) and were carried out in the free, open-source whole genome association analysis toolset PLINK (Purcell et al., 2007). Per-individual quality control included the exclusion of subjects with (a) missing genotype information of more than 5%, (b) excess heterozygosity (outliers defined as mean heterozygosity +/- 3SD), and (c) duplicated or related individuals (pairwise clustering based on identity-by-descent; exclusion based on an estimated pi cut-off> 0.1875). Seven subjects failed these tests. Sex was estimated based on SNP data and was in line with self-disclosure. The per-marker quality control excluded SNPs with (a) an excessive missing genotype rate of more than 10%, (b) significantly different missing genotype rates between cases and controls, and (c) a minor allele frequency below 5% (Anderson et al., 2010). 1,484 SNPs were removed. After imaging QC, our final sample included 239 participants (162 males, 77 females; 126 HC, 113 patients with SCZ) and 1,067,955 autosomal SNPs.

Statistics

PLINK was also used for testing the association between each of the 1,067,955 SNPs and left STG thickness. We applied an additive linear regression model including age, scanner field strength, diagnosis and gender as covariates. In order to obtain schizophrenia-specific results, we chose to analyze the SNP by diagnosis interaction effect (in addition to main effects of SNP and diagnosis which were also included in our model). As population substructure can inflate false positive results, especially in association studies using heterogeneous samples, we controlled for population stratification in the following way: EIGENSTRAT of the EIGENSOFT 3.0 software package (Patterson et al., 2006; Price et al., 2006) was used for the principal component analysis (PCA) of the genotype data. Before PCA, we pruned SNPs based on linkage disequilibrium (LD) as recommended (Laurie et al., 2010) and excluded autosomal SNPs as well as SNPs in problematic regions of long-range LD (Price et al., 2008). We extracted the first ten principal components and (based on the Tracy-Widom-Statistic) included the first four as additional covariates into the regression models (Hass et al., 2013; see SM Table 2.1).

To verify our results, we repeated the analyses in an ethnically more homogeneous subsample. For this purpose, four HapMap populations, Utah residents with ancestry from northern and western Europe (CEU), Han Chinese in Beijing (CHB), Japanese in Tokyo (JPT), and Yoruba in Ibadan, Nigeria (YRA) (International HapMap Project: http://www.hapmap.org/), were clustered with our pruned SNP set as defined above. A homogeneous subsample of individuals close to the CEU cluster (n = 170) (for more information see also SM 2.2) was selected based on the analysis in EIGENSTRAT (Hass et al., 2013).

VEGAS

Since gene-based approaches can be more powerful than traditional GWA studies, we used the gene-based analysis tool VEGAS (Liu et al., 2010) for further tests. VEGAS and other gene-based tests might detect additive effects of genetic variants with small effect sizes that would not be ascertainable in the ambient noise of GWA studies. Furthermore, VEGAS takes LD structure and other possible confounding factors such as gene size into account by using permutation in the computation procedure. All GWA study SNPs and their p-values were included in the analysis, resulting in 17,744 autosomal genes and the corresponding gene-based and lowest SNP p-values (for more information, see also SM 2.3).

Pathway approach

To detect underlying pathophysiological pathways involved in schizophrenia, all genes with an empirical gene-based p-value below 0.01 (127 genes) (Verweij et al., 2010) were included in a pathway analysis using the KEGG Pathway Database (Kanehisa and Goto, 2000; Kanehisa et al., 2014). More details can be found in the supplementary material (see SM 2.4).

GWA study

The interaction between each of the 1,067,955 SNPs and disease status for left STG thickness was tested using an additive regression model. No marker reached the genome-wide significance threshold of 5 × 10-8 (Figure 1; for a quantile-quantile plot see SM Figure 3.1). However, ten SNPs achieved p-values less than 10-6 (Table 2). SM Table 3.2 shows the distribution of genotypes, call rate and Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium for these ten SNPs. The strongest evidence for a SNP by diagnosis interaction effect was found for rs1587409 (p = 1.69 × 10-7) on chromosome 8. Rs12544116 (p = 3.86 ×10-7) and rs1499419 (p = 3.92 × 10-6), also on chromosome 8, were found to be in perfect LD with rs1587409 (see SM Figure 3.2).

Discussion

Summary of findings

We combined a gene-discovery approach with a pathway analysis to understand some of the genetics underlying STG thinning in schizophrenia. Even though we could not distinguish if our results constitute an etiological or consequential finding in terms of the relationship between SCZ and left STG thinning, by examining the SNP by diagnosis interaction effect for left STG thickness in a case-control sample, we found ten SNPs in six genomic regions showing a suggestive association (p-values greater than 10-8). Our strongest findings in the GWA study and pathway analysis point towards an involvement of glucose metabolism in left STG thickness reduction in patients with schizophrenia only. In another study by Ohi et al. (2014), no genome-wide significant variant influencing left STG volume was detected either, but they found a marginal interaction between diagnosis and a single polygenic schizophrenia score (PSS) on left STG volume. Whereas the PSS was significantly negatively correlated with the local gray matter in the left STG in patients (i.e., higher PSSs were associated with smaller left STG volumes), no effect of the score on left STG volume was seen in HCs. The PSS explained approximately 3.2% of the variance in the total left STG volume in patients with SCZ.

Top hits/pathways and their relation to schizophrenia

Three out of our ten top SNPs are located in the genomic region of SLC30A8, which is a pancreatic beta-cell specific zinc transporter involved in the synthesis, storage and secretion of insulin (Chimienti et al., 2005). The gene has previously been linked to type 2 diabetes (Nicolson et al., 2009; Dupuis et al., 2010; Voight et al., 2010; Strawbridge et al., 2011), a disease which is known to show an increased prevalence in patients with schizophrenia compared to the general population (Heiskanen et al., 2003; Henderson, 2005; De Hert et al., 2009; Meyer and Stahl, 2009).

Furthermore, we showed an enrichment of our tested genes (see supplemental material) in five pathways including insulin, calcium, PI3K-Akt and MAPK signaling, as well as other metabolic pathways. Whereas malfunctions in the calcium, PI3K-Akt and MAPK signaling pathways have also been described in patients with schizophrenia (Lidow, 2003; Zheng et al., 2012; Funk et al., 2012), the insulin signaling pathway is of special interest considering that our top associations were located near a gene which is also involved in the glucose-insulin cycle.

Evidence for alterations of calcium signaling in patients with schizophrenia has been demonstrated previously (Lidow, 2003); several studies (Green et al., 2010; Schizophrenia Working Group of the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium, 2014; Jiang et al., 2015) have shown the involvement of a central gene of the pathway, CACNA1C, in the pathophysiology of schizophrenia. Whereas the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway is involved in cell growth and cell proliferation as well as in the metabolism of cells, the MAP-Kinase signaling pathway is a multilevel signal transduction pathway which interacts with cellular processes such as cell differentiation, cell growth and programmed cell death.

Top hits and superior temporal gyrus

Insulin release, insulin signaling and glucose utilization contribute all to energy metabolism homeostasis. Interestingly, Martins-de-Souza et al. (2009) reported the up-regulation (14 genes) and down-regulation (11 genes) of the expression of 553 proteins, most of which are involved in energy metabolism, in a proteome analysis of the STG of patients with schizophrenia. Given these metabolic abnormalities and based on our findings, the involvement of the insulin signaling pathway in cortical thickness reduction of the superior temporal gyrus in patients with schizophrenia is not unlikely and might even suggest that patients with schizophrenia and a manifest comorbid type 2 diabetes mellitus (or at least high glucose levels) experience a faster superior temporal gyrus shrinking.

Potential risk mechanisms

A GWA study in a French case-control cohort identified the SLC30A8 gene as a risk locus for type 2 diabetes (Sladek et al., 2007). As we emphasized before, patients with schizophrenia suffer more often from comorbid conditions such as metabolic syndrome, a complex of diseases including diabetes type 2, which is characterized by an altered insulin sensitivity and glucose tolerance. This relationship is not just found in medicated patients but also, to a lesser extent though, in unmedicated and first-episode patients (Kohen, 2004; Verma et al., 2009; Cohen and De Hert, 2011). Although there was no overlap in genetic risk variants for schizophrenia and type 2 diabetes mellitus, Liu et al. (2013) documented a pathogenetic association between these two diseases on a pathway level. Based on the idea that insulin signaling deficits may underlie schizophrenia, Altar et al. (2008) successfully demonstrated that stimulation of muscarinic and insulin/IGF-1 receptors in human neuroblastoma and astrocyte cell lines modulate genes associated with metabolic and synaptic functions opposite to their change reported in schizophrenia brain.

Glucose is known as the main fuel for all body cells including neurons and glial cells. Glucose is the starting product for glutamate; therefore increased blood glucose levels which can be found in carriers of particular SLC30A8 gene variants (Ruchat et al., 2009), can result in excessive brain glutamate. High glutamate levels may be responsible for physiological and anatomical changes in the brain such as hyperexcitability, alterations in synaptic plasticity and excitotoxicity (Mark et al., 2001). The latter might result in neuronal atrophy and loss of neuropil through cell damage and death, resulting in reduced cortical thickness (McGeer and McGeer, 1976). High glutamate levels can also account for some of the typical schizophrenia symptoms such as hallucinations (Braithwaite et al., 2013). However, considering that not all schizophrenia patients show this pattern, it is possible that there is an underlying metabolic vulnerability in a subset of individuals only, which interacts with environmental or genetic factors.

Limitations

Our findings should be interpreted in the light of the following limitations: Firstly, none of the SNPs reached the genome-wide significance threshold. However, we found associations for four SNPs with p-values of 10-7. Secondly, our sample was medium-sized and rather heterogeneous; however, we did find supporting evidence for our results in a more homogeneous subsample of individuals of European descent only. Thirdly, the cause of cortical atrophy in the STG is unknown in patients with SCZ and might be due to accidental, etiological or consequential alterations. Whereas there is some evidence for a strong relationship between cerebral glucose metabolism and brain atrophy in healthy volunteers (Yoshii et al., 1988), Ibáñez et al., (1998) showed that regional glucose metabolic abnormalities are not the results of atrophy in patients with Alzheimer's disease. Further research is warranted to clarify the relationship between reduced brain metabolism (and utilization of glucose in particular) and an atrophic brain/cortex, especially in patients with SCZ. Fourthly, we could not formally test our interpretations regarding schizophrenia and diabetes, since our sample did not include any diabetes relevant data such as blood insulin levels or fasting glucose levels. However, we found a negative correlation between high body mass index (BMI), which is just as type 2 diabetes one of the metabolic syndrome symptoms, and left STG thickness in patients only (r = -0.214, p = 0.024; in HC: r = -0.135, p = 0.131; the correlation was not driven by patients with a higher cumulative antipsychotic dose). However, to clarify the relationship between diabetes and schizophrenia and to elucidate the function of the insulin signaling pathway in schizophrenia, further studies are warranted. This relationship might also be explained by an unhealthy lifestyle, which is more common in patients with schizophrenia than in controls (Brown et al., 1999), and the use of (especially atypical) antipsychotic drugs which promote weight gain and other manifestations of the metabolic syndrome such as impaired fasting glucose levels. Antipsychotic medication can even facilitate the manifestation of type 2 diabetes in patients with schizophrenia (Newcomer, 2005). The fact that people with different genetic variants (e.g., alleles with bases “T” versus “G” for our top hit rs1587409) can respond differentially to environmental variation (e.g. medication) might also account for some of the phenotypical variance (e.g. left superior temporal gyrus thickness). This phenomenon is also called gene-environment interaction. To be clear, if reduced left STG thickness in schizophrenia is due to treatment with medication in patients with certain genetic variants, this would lend support to the hypothesis that the genetic variation alone is not a primary risk factor for schizophrenia but it is rather the gene-environment interaction which leads to the emergence of symptoms and chronicity. However, we did not find a significant interaction between genetic variation and dosage of antipsychotic drugs in our sample. Lastly, medication can confound thickness measures in schizophrenia patients (van Haren et al., 2011; Lesh et al., 2015). However, here the dosage of typical or atypical antipsychotic medication that patients were receiving at the time of the scan was not correlated with left STG cortical thickness measures in our sample (ps = 0.947 and 0.426, respectively). This result is consistent with the finding of Nesvåg et al. (2008) who showed that current dose of antipsychotic medication does not significantly affect variation in cortical thickness.

Conclusion

Taken together, we found supporting evidence for a genetic association between left STG thickness, an endophenotype for schizophrenia, and schizophrenia-relevant biochemical pathways such as the insulin signaling pathway. The results of this study, which were schizophrenia-specific through the tested interaction effect, may further our understanding of the underlying genetics of this burdensome psychiatric disorder, and may help to identify potential risk mechanisms for schizophrenia as well as to untangle the relationship of schizophrenia with comorbidities such as the metabolic syndrome.
