Abstract

Context

Cortical thickness is a highly heritable structural brain measurement and reduced thickness has been associated with both schizophrenia and bipolar disorder as well as decreased cognitive performance among healthy controls. Identifying genes that contribute to variation in cortical thickness provides a path to elucidate some of the biological mechanisms underlying these diseases as well as general cognitive abilities.

Objective

To identify common genetic variants that affect cortical thickness in schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and controls and secondarily to test these variants for association with cognitive performance.

Design

597,198 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) were tested for association with average cortical thickness in a genome-wide association study (GWAS). Significantly associated SNPs were tested for their affect on several measures of cognitive performance.

Setting

Four major hospitals in Oslo, Norway.

Participants

The GWAS included controls (n = 181) and individuals with DSM-IV diagnosed schizophrenia spectrum disorder (n = 94), bipolar spectrum disorder (n = 97), and other psychotic and affective disorders (n = 49). The follow-up cognitive study included an additional 622 cases and controls.

Main Outcome Measures

Cortical thickness measured with magnetic resonance imaging and cognitive performance as assessed by several neuropsychological tests.

Results

Two closely linked genetic variants (rs4906844 and rs11633924) within the Prader-Willi/Angelman syndrome region on chromosome 15q12 showed genome-wide significant association (p = 1.08 × 10−8) with average cortical thickness as well as modest association with cognitive performance (p = 0.028) specifically among subjects diagnosed with schizophrenia.

Conclusion

This is the first GWAS to identify a common genetic variant that contributes to the heritable reduction of cortical thickness in schizophrenia. These results highlight the utility of cortical thickness as an intermediate phenotype for neuropsychiatric diseases. Future independent replication studies are required to confirm these findings.

Introduction

Schizophrenia is recognized as one of the leading causes of morbidity around the world with a life time risk of approximately 1% worldwide1, and ranks as one of the most costly disorders to afflict humans2. The heritability of schizophrenia is high, estimated in most studies to be approximately 80%3–5. Despite this fact, the mechanisms underlying susceptibility to schizophrenia remain elusive, likely due to both the genetic and phenotypic heterogeneity of the disorder. The analysis of endophenotypes or ‘intermediate’ phenotypes has great potential to shed light on the genetic mechanisms underlying schizophrenia and other potentially related neuropsychiatric disorders, such as bipolar disorder. The power of this analytic strategy is derived from the observation that endophenotypes may reflect underlying pathologies more proximal to genetic lesions and may help create more homogenous subtypes of schizophrenia that are amenable to genetic analysis6, 7. Therefore, we pursued a genome-wide association study (GWAS) leveraging variation in an endophenotype derived from structural brain measures that is thought to reflect a component of pathogenetic processes leading to, or associated with, schizophrenia.

Genetic association analyses focusing on the role of common DNA sequence variation and schizophrenia diagnosis without leveraging endophenotypes or an attempt to accommodate the likely heterogeneity associated with schizophrenia have been highly problematic. Indeed, only recently genome-wide association studies have identified common single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) that show a reproducible association with the disease, but these SNPs have only moderate risk and odds ratios, ~1.1–1.58, 9. In contrast, the use of endophenotypes has resulted in some successes. For example, candidate gene studies have identified SNP associations with both anatomical10–12 and functional13 brain measures, many of which can be considered intermediate phenotypes for schizophrenia. In addition, a recent GWAS identified SNPs associated with brain activation in schizophrenia14. While these candidate gene associations require further replication, they support the claim that common genetic variation can be associated with neural intermediate phenotypes of relevance to schizophrenia. Therefore, we undertook a genome-wide association study (GWAS) approach to identify genes related to a specific structural brain measurement, cortical thickness, thought to play a role in mediating schizophrenia15–19 and potentially related disorders.

We have previously reported both similarities and differences in cortical and subcortical brain structures between schizophrenia and bipolar disorders20. The similarities between the disorders with respect to neural phenotypes call into question strict diagnostic differences between them. In fact, traditional definitions of schizophrenia and bipolar disorder are being reevaluated by the psychiatric and research communities21, and there is now increasing evidence that supports overlapping pathogenetic mechanisms and genetic susceptibility for these two disorders22–26. Schizophrenia and bipolar disorder may therefore be perceived as dimensionally different, or different in the degrees to which specific substrates or neural mechanisms are perturbed, rather than as categorically separate disease entities27. Our study, which includes individuals with both schizophrenia and bipolar disorder, aims to identify neuronal and molecular mechanisms specific for schizophrenia and thus will help highlight distinct features of this disorder.

We focus specifically on cortical thickness as an appropriate endophenotype because this brain measure is highly heritabile28, and also because widespread cortical thinning is consistently seen in first episode and chronic schizophrenia15–19. In addition, cortical thickness reduction is associated with lower cognitive performance in both healthy and diseased populations, including decreases on several neuropsychological tests of executive function that have been observed among individuals with neuropsychiatric conditions27, 29, 30. Therefore, our GWAS was pursued not just to identify common SNPs that are associated with variation in cortical thickness in schizophrenia, but also to contrast these SNP effects among individuals with bipolar disorder as well as among the subjects independent of diagnosis. We also tested associated SNPs with cognitive performance among the patient groups.

Methods

Subjects

1043 participants from the Thematically Organized Psychoses (TOP) study were analyzed and included 481 controls, 345 diagnosed with schizophrenia spectrum disorder (SCZ), 179 with bipolar spectrum disorder (BD), 13 with major depressive disorder (MDD), and 36 diagnosed with a psychotic disorder not otherwise specified (PDNOS). SCZ diagnoses included schizophrenia, schizoaffective, and schizophreniform disorders, and BD diagnoses included bipolar I, bipolar II, and bipolar disorder not otherwise specified.

The clinically diagnosed participants were recruited continuously from psychiatric units in four major hospitals in Oslo, Norway. Trained psychiatrists and clinical psychologists carried out clinical assessments. Diagnosis was based on the Structured Clinical Interview for Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fourth edition (DSM-IV) Axis I disorders (SCID-I)31. The healthy control sample was randomly selected from the same catchment area and screened with an interview about severe mental illness and the Primary Care Evaluation of Mental Disorders (PRIME-MD)32.

All analyses in this study included subsets of the 1043 subjects from the TOP study. Some subjects were included in all analyses, while others were included in only one analysis, depending on the imaging, genotype, and cognitive data available for each subject. 421 subjects (181 controls, 94 SCZ, 97 BD, 49 MDD/PDNOS) had both genotype data and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans. These 421 subjects were included in the genome-wide association study (GWAS) with average cortical thickness as the phenotype of interest.

754 subjects (368 controls, 208 SCZ, 178 BD), including 371 subjects from the initial GWAS, had cognitive and appropriate genotype data and were included in the follow-up association analyses involving cognitive performance as the phenotype of interest. 826 subjects (481 controls and 345 SCZ) had cognitive measurements (but not necessarily genotype data) and were used to estimate the effect of schizophrenia diagnosis on cognition (see Table 2 and eTable 2; for details see Rimol et al.20 and Simonsen et al.27). Schizophrenia symptom severity, as assessed by the Positive and Negative Symptoms Scale (PANSS), was comparable in the three groups of schizophrenia subjects used in the GWAS (total score 60.8 ± 15.4), cognitive and genotype association (61.9 ± 15.0), and cognitive analysis (63.2 ± 16.2). Subjects in each diagnostic group across analyses included approximately equal numbers of men and women, were similar ages (range 18–65), and had comparable IQs (see Table 1 for details).

Genotyping

DNA was extracted from blood and genotyped on the Affymetrix 6.0 array, as previously reported33, 34. All TOP subjects self-reported Norwegian ancestry. 597,198 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) passed quality control filters (call rate > 95%, minor allele frequency > 5%, Hardy-Weinberg disequilibrium P < 10−6) and were merged with HapMap 3 reference populations. Principal component analysis (PCA) of an allele sharing distance matrix across all subjects did not suggest any genetic outliers.

Imputation

TOP genotypes were merged with the HapMap CEU reference population, which also included genetic variant information from the sequencing by the 1000 Genomes project. MACH was used to impute genotypes with the default settings, and only SNPs that passed imputation quality control (R > 0.5) were included for further analysis.

Imaging

MRI scans were performed with a 1.5T Siemens Magnetom Sonata scanner equipped with a standard head coil. Acquisition parameters were optimized for increased gray/white matter image contrast. Brains were segmented with the FreeSurfer software package (http://freesurfer-software.org). For more details, see also Rimol et al.20.

Neurocognitive assessment

Psychologists trained in standardized neuropsychological testing carried out neurocognitive assessment. A three-hour test battery (including measures of estimated premorbid IQ and adequate test effort) was administered in a fixed order with two breaks with refreshments. For this analysis, we focused on 17 measures from three tests that made various demands on executive function and other cognitive domains. Among those measures included were Letter Fluency, Category Fluency, Category Switching, and Repetition and Set-Loss Error components from the Delis-Kaplan Executive Function System (D-KEFS)35 Verbal Fluency Test. Additional measures included were Repetitions and Intrusions, Long Delay Free Recall, and Semantic, Subjective, and Serial Clustering components of the California Verbal Learning Test (CVLT-II)36, and the Block Design, Matrix Reasoning, and Similarities components of the Weschler Abbreviated Scale of Intelligence (WASI)37. See Table 2 and eTable 3 for details.

Statistics

In each diagnostic group, we computed the mean, standard deviation, skew, and kurtosis of average cortical thickness measurements. We then tested whether these measurements deviated significantly from a Gaussian distribution with both a D'Agostino-Pearson omnibus K2 and a Shapiro-Wilks test of normality (eTable 1). For each SNP tested for association, we used PLINK38 to fit an additive linear model with minor allele count, sex, and age (and diagnosis in the combined subject analysis) as predictors of average cortical thickness. For the most significantly associated SNP in schizophrenia, we fit a linear model with minor allele count, sex, and age as predictors of cortical thickness for each brain vertex across the cortical surface and generated a cortical map based on SNP −log10(p-values). In addition, we divided schizophrenia subjects into three groups based on rs4906844 genotype, combined each group with control subjects, and fit a linear model with diagnosis, sex, and age as predictors of cortical thickness across the cortical surface. We generated a cortical map based on diagnosis −log10(p-values) for each genotype group.

Multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) was run with the R statistical software package39 to test the association of the SNP most strongly associated with cortical thickness, rs4906844, with cognitive phenotypes among schizophrenia subjects, while controlling for age and sex. Subject labels were randomly shuffled 10,000 times, the MANOVA was repeated, and a permuted p-value was computed based on the number of times Pillai’s trace statistic was greater than the statistic from the non-permuted data. Post-hoc linear regressions were run to test the association of this SNP with individual cognitive measures, while controlling for age and sex, and permutation-based p-values were obtained. This analysis was repeated in a much larger combined sample of schizophrenia and control subjects to test the effect of diagnosis on these cognitive tests. For details see Rimol et al.20 and Simonsen et al.27. A sign test (equivalent to a binomial test with probability equal to 0.5) was used to test the significance of the similarity in directions of the SNP and diagnosis effects on the cognitive tests. The Broad Institute’s SNAP online plotting tool40 was used to generate the SNP association figure.

Results

We tested each SNP in the GWAS with average cortical thickness in each of three subject groups: (i) schizophrenia spectrum disorder (n = 94); (ii) bipolar spectrum disorder (n = 97); and (iii) all subjects in our study (n = 421) including healthy controls (n = 181), schizophrenia (studied in i) and bipolar disorder (studied in ii), and subjects with other psychotic and affective disorders (n = 49). Cortical thickness measurements in each diagnostic group could not be distinguished from a Gaussian distribution based on two tests of normality (see eTable 1 for details) and were therefore not transformed for the GWAS. We controlled for the effects of age and sex and set a conservative Bonferroni corrected-threshold for genome-wide significance (p = 5 × 10−8 / 3 = 1.67 × 10−8) to accommodate our analyses involving 3 different subsets of subjects, as noted above.

In the schizophrenia subject group, two SNPs (rs4906844 and rs11633924) were significantly associated (p = 1.08 × 10−8; β = −0.069 mm per minor allele; n = 94) with average cortical thickness (Fig. 1 and eFigs. 1 and 2), while in the second and third subject groups, no SNP associations were genome-wide significant (eFigs. 3 and 4). Since these two SNPs are perfectly correlated in our sample of schizophrenia subjects (r2 = 1.0), we arbitrarily focused the rest of our analysis on one SNP (rs4906844). This analysis is consistent with results for closely linked SNPs, including rs11633924. The fact that two SNPs on the genotyping chip exhibited association and were in strong linkage disequilibrium suggests that our association was not likely to result from a genotyping artifact. We find that schizophrenia subjects with zero copies of the minor allele of SNP rs4906844 (genotype GG, n = 26) have an average cortical thickness of 2.27 ± 0.09 mm; subjects with one copy (AG, n = 45) have an average thickness of 2.20 ± 0.09 mm; and subjects with two copies of the minor allele (AA, n = 23) have an average thickness of 2.13 ± 0.09 mm. These results represent a 3% reduction in cortical thickness per copy of the minor allele and a strikingly large effect size based on the comparison of schizophrenia subjects homozygous for the major and minor alleles (Cohen’s d = (2.27 – 2.13) / 0.09 = 1.56). The three genotypic groups of schizophrenia subjects did not significantly differ in age (p = 0.72), full-scale IQ (p = 0.59), or symptom severity (p = 0.93) as assessed by the Positive and Negative Symptoms Scale (PANSS) based on one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) tests. Also, the groups did not differ in the proportion of female subjects (p = 0.53) based on a Fisher’s exact test.

rs4906844 is associated with a widespread reduction in cortical thickness that is more prominent in frontotemporal regions (Fig. 2 and eTable 2) and is remarkably similar to the pattern of cortical thinning that has been consistently observed in schizophrenia versus healthy controls15–17. We tested whether rs4906844 can account for this pattern of thinning by grouping schizophrenia subjects by genotype and, for each genotypic group, comparing cortical thickness between this subset of schizophrenia subjects and all controls. (Note that we did not group the control subjects by genotype because we found no significant SNP effect on cortical thickness among controls.) We find that schizophrenia subjects homozygous for the minor allele (AA) show the most significant thinning, while heterozygotes (AG) show moderate thinning, and homozygotes for the major allele (GG) show no significant thinning relative to controls (Fig. 3). Therefore, this SNP explains virtually all of the observed difference in cortical thickness between schizophrenia and controls in our study sample, although it does not account for other structural differences, such as reduced brain and subcortical volumes in schizophrenia.

In order to characterize the specificity of the SNP effect on cortical thickness, we tested the association of rs4906844 in two other diagnostic groups. We found that the SNP was not significantly associated with average cortical thickness in subjects with bipolar disorder (p = 0.88; β = −0.002; n = 97) or healthy controls (p = 0.13; β = 0.015; n = 181) despite similar sample sizes and thus comparable power to detect an association (Fig. 4). Furthermore, in a combined sample of schizophrenia and control subjects, the SNP × diagnosis interaction p-value is significant (1.56 × 10−7), but less significant than the initial finding among subjects with schizophrenia. In addition, the SNP effect is specific to cortical thickness, as there is no significant association between this SNP and other brain measures including cortical surface area and intracranial, whole brain, subcortical, cerebellar, and ventricular volumes.

Finally, we tested whether rs4906844 is associated with increased risk for schizophrenia. We find that rs4906844 minor allele frequency (MAF) in schizophrenia (0.47) and control subjects (0.48) is not significantly different (p = 0.91), and the MAF is also comparable in subjects with bipolar disorder (0.42).

Since cortical thickness has been associated with cognitive performance in healthy adults and schizophrenia29, 30, 41, we tested rs4906844 for association with cognition. Cognitive performance is significantly less heritable than brain structural measures and is associated with many neural factors not measured by cortical thickness, including cortical area and functional connectivity42. Therefore, the effect of a single SNP on cognition is likely to be small. Thus, in order to increase our power to detect an effect, we leveraged a multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) test of the association of rs4906844 with 17 tasks from the following tests: Weschler Abbreviated Scale of Intelligence (WASI)37, California Verbal Learning Test (CVLT-II)36, and the Delis-Kaplan Executive Function System (D-KEFS)35 (Table 2). These tasks assess higher-level executive skills with varying degrees of specificity, and task performance has been shown to correlate with structure and function of the cortex29, 30, 43.

The cognitive analysis included 196 schizophrenia subjects who had both SNP genotype data and a complete set of measurements for all 17 neurocognitive measures. Briefly, MANOVA tests the association of the 17 measure profile with genotype by assessing the probability that a linear combination of these measures discriminates subjects based on the number of minor alleles of rs4906844 that they carried purely by chance. We used permutation tests to calculate the significance of the association. We controlled for the effect of age and sex in the MANOVA test, and we found that rs4906844 was significantly associated (permuted p = 0.028; Pillai = 0.15) with the phenotypic cognitive performance profile composed of the 17 measures, as described above.

We hypothesized that this SNP association with cognition was mediated by the SNP effect on cortical thickness. In order to test this hypothesis, we performed a secondary analysis with the subset of schizophrenia subjects (n = 86) from the initial GWAS that had imaging, genotype, and cognitive data. A MANOVA test identified rs4906844 as significantly associated with the cognitive performance profile (p = 0.0077; Pillai = 0.37), although this association was no longer significant (p = 0.14; Pillai = 0.28) when cortical thickness was included as a covariate. Moreover, a MANOVA test confirmed our prediction that rs4906844 is not associated with cognitive performance in subjects with bipolar disorder (p = 0.49; Pillai = 0.10; n = 178) or healthy controls (p = 0.39; Pillai = 0.07; n = 368).

We performed post-hoc linear regression tests for the association between rs4906844 minor allele count and each cognitive test measure in a slightly larger set of schizophrenia subjects (sample size range: 201–208) who had measurements for each test. Specifically, we predicted that the 14 cognitive tests that revealed significantly decreased performance in schizophrenia subjects versus healthy controls would also reveal decreased performance in schizophrenia subjects with more copies of the SNP minor allele. Indeed, we find that the SNP is associated with nominally decreased performance on 11 of the 14 cognitive tests, and collectively these 11 tests involve more tests than would be expected by chance to occur based on a sign test if the measures are independent (p = 0.029). None of the SNP associations are statistically significant after correcting for multiple comparisons; however the directions of the SNP effects are consistent with our hypothesis that reduced cortical thickness is associated with decreased cognitive performance.

On average, the effect size per minor allele on test performance in schizophrenia is about 6% of the effect size of schizophrenia diagnosis (Table 2). For example, schizophrenia subjects homozygous for the minor allele (AA, n = 46) perform 0.1–0.2 standard deviations below schizophrenia subjects homozygous for the major allele (GG, n = 58) on the Block Design, Matrix Reasoning, and Similarities measures of the WASI test. By contrast, schizophrenia subjects as a whole perform about 0.8 standard deviations worse on these three WASI tests than healthy controls. These results suggest that this SNP explains a small but consistent portion of the cognitive deficits that are associated with schizophrenia. Intriguingly, the most significant SNP association (nominal p = 0.026) is associated with a reduction in the measure of Total Repetition Errors on the D-KEFS Verbal Fluency Test that accounts for the entire difference between schizophrenia subjects and healthy controls.

The SNP rs4906844 is located in the second intron of a putative protein-coding gene (LOC100128714) on chromosome 15q12 near (< 500kb) two maternally imprinted genes (ATP10A and UBE3A) and one non-imprinted gene (GABRB3) (Fig. 1). Paternal or maternal deficiencies in the large (several megabase) region spanning rs4906844 are associated with Prader-Willi and Angelman syndromes, respectively.

We imputed SNPs in the region using MACH with genotypes from the HapMap CEU reference population and tested these SNPs for association with cortical thickness in schizophrenia. 21 SNPs were significantly associated (P < 1 × 10−7) with average cortical thickness and are closely linked (r2 > 0.7) to rs4906844. These SNPs span the last two exons and the 3' untranslated region (3'-UTR) of LOC100128714. Five imputed SNPs show more significant association (8.3 × 10−9 < P < 9.3 × 10−9) with cortical thickness than rs4906844 and are within 3.5kb downstream of this SNP. A haplotype analysis did not reveal any haplotype blocks with a stronger association than the top SNPs, and no SNPs showed a significant association with cortical thickness when rs4906844 was included as a covariate. These results suggest that rs4906844 or one of the closely linked SNPs nearby is the functionally relevant genetic variant, or else is the best available proxy for this functional variant.

Discussion

In this study, we have identified a common genetic variant that is associated with cortical thickness with genome-wide significance (p = 1.1 × 10−8) specifically in schizophrenia. This SNP (rs4906844) accounts for virtually all of the difference in cortical thickness between a sample of schizophrenia subjects and healthy controls, although the SNP does not explain other neuroanatomical differences between these groups, including reduced volumes of the cerebellum, hippocampus, thalamus, and amygdala20. In addition, rs4906844 is modestly associated with neurocognitive performance among subjects diagnosed with schizophrenia, likely due to variation in cortical thickness associated with this SNP. Furthermore, the specificity of these associations for schizophrenia suggests differences in the genetic architecture that shapes brain structure in bipolar disorder.

Our finding that rs4906844 does not increase risk for schizophrenia is consistent with the observation that cortical thinning is not a strong intermediate phenotype for this disease. While healthy adolescent siblings of subjects with childhood onset schizophrenia have thinner cortices than controls44, this difference disappears by age 20, and healthy adult relatives of schizophrenia subjects show only trend-level cortical thinning compared to controls15, 18, 44. Therefore, thinning may be related more to (genetically and environmentally-mediated) disease processes and less to a genetic liability for schizophrenia.

rs4906844 may interact with these disease processes to alter cortical thickness, possibly via epistasis with SNPs associated with schizophrenia or via interactions with ‘environmental’ conditions characteristic of schizophrenia, such as use of anti-psychotic medications and altered neurocognitive processing. Alternatively, rs4906844 may act earlier to reduce cortical thickness and contribute directly to schizophrenia onset because of the increased vulnerability of the brain to abnormal development. Various environmental challenges may contribute to this vulnerability, including living in an urban area1 or being an immigrant45, both possible indicators of psychosocial adversity, as well as fetal risks1. In either case, rs4906844 would be expected to show no association with cortical thickness in subjects without a schizophrenia diagnosis, and this is what we observe in this study. However, future studies will be needed to explore the specificity of the SNP association on cortical thickness in other patient groups with heritable cortical abnormalities, such as autism.

Cortical thinning is associated with decreased cognitive performance29, 30, 41 so it is important to identify genes that contribute to variation in cortical thickness in health and disease. rs4906844 is significantly associated with a multivariate profile implicating 17 neurocognitive tests that make various demands on executive skills. Moreover, this association is no longer significant if cortical thickness is included as a covariate, suggesting that the SNP effect on cognition is mediated by its effect on cortical structure. As predicted, rs4906844 is significantly associated with nominally reduced performance on the vast majority of cognitive tests, and the SNP is nominally associated with decreased Total Repetition Errors on the D-KEFS Verbal Fluency Test. These results are consistent with a recent study in an independent set of subjects that demonstrated significant associations between several measures of executive function and cortical thinning in both schizophrenia and healthy controls29.

However, schizophrenia subjects homozygous for the major allele, who showed no significant cortical thinning relative to control subjects, performed significantly worse on most cognitive tests than controls, emphasizing the importance of factors not captured by differences in cortical thickness that contribute to decreased cognitive performance in schizophrenia. A limitation of this study is that some of the neurocognitive measures included in the analysis probe cognitive domains beyond executive function. For example, both the CVLT-II and D-KEFS tests make demands on verbal ability, and the Block Design measure of the WASI test is sensitive to deficits in visuospatial and motor skills. Therefore, the SNP association we have found may be driven in part by deficits in these other cognitive areas.

rs4906844 is located within the putative gene LOC100128714 that is expressed in human brain and codes for an amino acid sequence that is > 96% conserved in great apes. The predicted protein structure contains no domains homologous to proteins with known function. LOC100128714 lies within the Prader-Willi and Angelman syndrome deletion region on chromosome 15q11–q13, and Prader-Willi syndrome is sometimes characterized by psychotic features, particularly in those subjects with maternal uniparental disomy of chromosome 1546, 47. Maternally-derived duplications of 15q11–13 may also be a rare risk factor for schizophrenia and related psychoses48. LOC100128714 has the potential to regulate one of the many imprinted and non-imprinted genes at this locus (including proximal genes ATP10A, UBE3A, and GABRB3) that have been implicated in neurodevelopment and neuropsychiatric disease49. For example, Ube3A has recently been implicated in regulating excitatory synapse development by targeting for degradation the synaptic proteins Arc50 and Ephexin551. If rs4906844 indirectly regulates Ube3A, thereby altering synaptic development, then this SNP could contribute to the decreased dendritic spine density and resulting reductions to neuropil volume that have been associated with cortical thinning in schizophrenia52.

Previous work in schizophrenia identified an enrichment of rare microdeletions on chromosome 15q11.2 and 15q13.3 several megabases proximal and distal, respectively, to the region found in this study55. Autism spectrum disorders have also been associated with 15q11-13; duplication of this genomic region may account for up to 2% of cases49, 53, and a small 1.4 megabase de novo duplication has been identified in one autistic subject that spans LOC100128714, ATP10A, and GABRB354. The coincidence of copy number variations (CNVs) associated with schizophrenia and autism on chromosome 15q11-13 and across the genome suggests an overlapping genomic and developmental etiology of these neuropsychiatric disorders56, 57. It will be interesting to see whether rs4906844 or other SNPs in this region contribute to cortical phenotypes in autism.

Several GWAS have identified a handful of genetic loci consistently associated with schizophrenia8, 9, but no SNPs have been implicated on chromosome 15q12. Consistent with these results, we find no association between rs4906844 and schizophrenia diagnosis. Rather, we have identified a SNP that explains some of the neuroanatomical and cognitive heterogeneity within schizophrenia. Future studies should consider sequencing this genomic region in a large cohort of subjects with schizophrenia in order to search for rare causal variants, and studies should explore the expression and imprinting of LOC100128714 as well as the function of the resulting protein.
