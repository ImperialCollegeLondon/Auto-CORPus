Meta-analysis identifies a MECOM gene as a novel predisposing factor of osteoporotic fracture

Abstract

Background

Osteoporotic fracture (OF) as a clinical endpoint is a major complication of osteoporosis. To screen for OF susceptibility genes, we performed a genome-wide association study and carried out de novo replication analysis of an East Asian population.

Methods

Association was tested using a logistic regression analysis. A meta-analysis was performed on the combined results using effect size and standard errors estimated for each study.

Results

In a combined meta-analysis of a discovery cohort (288 cases and 1139 controls), three hospital based sets in replication stage I (462 cases and 1745 controls), and an independent ethnic group in replication stage II (369 cases and 560 for controls), we identified a new locus associated with OF (rs784288 in the MECOM gene) that showed genome-wide significance (p=3.59×10−8; OR 1.39). RNA interference revealed that a MECOM knockdown suppresses osteoclastogenesis.

Conclusions

Our findings provide new insights into the genetic architecture underlying OF in East Asians.

INTRODUCTION

With the increasing number of aged persons in modern societies, osteoporotic fracture (OF) has become a cause of substantial morbidity and mortality.1 The annual costs of treating OF are predicted to increase by 50%, mainly due to the increased number of postmenopausal women.2–4 There is a substantial genetic contribution to OF in addition to low bone mineral density (BMD) and the heritability of this condition is therefore relatively high.5–7 Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) of osteoporosis have successfully identified predominant genetic variants for BMD in Eurocentric populations.8–15 The OF risk increases as BMD values fall and osteoporosis is thus clinically defined by the measurement of BMD, the most widely used predictor for fracture risk.16 Although BMD is useful as a surrogate marker for OF, about two thirds of individuals who suffer a fracture do not have osteoporosis on the basis of their BMD values.17 This indicates that identifying genetic factors responsible for OF will be very valuable. Despite identification of novel genes or loci, GWAS are fundamentally limited in terms of their ethnic specificity and ability to establish functional implications. Here, we describe genetic factors that predispose to OF in East Asian populations, and provide functional evidence that a newly identified gene may have a critical role in bone cells.

METHODS

Study subjects

The general characteristics of our study cohort and the distribution of clinical properties within cohort populations are listed in table 1. Patients from the Korean Genome Epidemiologic Study project of the Korea Association REsource (KARE) were recruited. A total of 10 038 participants, 40–69 years old, were registered at the first stage of the survey, performed in 2001–2002. Details of this study population have been recently described.18 We used low trauma fracture events to distinguish between fracture and non-fracture groups of women of the KARE study. Fractures were only included if they had occurred with low trauma (eg, fall from standing height or less) at any of six sites (hip, wrist, humerus, rib, pelvis, and vertebra) after the age of 40 years. Fractures clearly caused by high trauma events (eg, motor vehicle accidents, violence, or falls from more than the standing height of the individual) were excluded.

The KARE study is an ongoing prospective study involving a biennial examination. Subjects are followed for a mean of 46.3±2.2 months at the time of analysis. Heights are also measured at the follow-up period. A height loss of ≥4.0 cm during the follow-up period was also regarded as indicative of a new vertebral fracture (VF). A threshold of 4.0 cm has been shown to have a specificity of 98.3% and a positive predictive value of 63.9% for incident osteoporotic VFs (OVFs).19 Non-VF events are recorded at the first examination and biennially using a standardised self-administered questionnaire. Subjects with a history of malignancy or receiving any drug, such as anti-osteoporotic or other drugs, systemic glucocorticoids, or hormone therapies that might affect bone metabolism, were also excluded. Among the KARE cohort,18 we identified 288 women, aged 40–69 years, with OF. As a control group, we randomly selected up to four times the number of age, body mass index (BMI), and menopausal status matched non-fracture subjects (n=1139).

The replication cohorts comprised the Asan, Catholic and Kangwon Medical Center (ACK-MC) cohort in Korea and the Rikagaku Kenkyo (RIKEN) cohort in Japan. Patients with fractures caused by high trauma events were also excluded from the replication cohorts. In addition, we limited events to radiologically proven OVFs to specify OF more accurately in the ACK-MC and RIKEN cohorts. The Asan Medical Center (AMC) cohort comprised 973 postmenopausal Korean women who visited the AMC in Seoul.20 The Catholic Medical Center (CMC) cohort consisted of 653 participants in the Catholic Metabolic Disease Cohort Study, a longitudinal cohort study which began in 2003, based in Chungju suburb community.21 The Kangwon Medical Center (KMC) cohort consisted of 581 postmenopausal Korean women who visited the KMC.22 Subjects were selected from the ACK-MC cohort by applying the same criteria used for the KARE cohort subjects. Except for the KMC cohort, age and BMI matched controls were randomly selected for fracture cases, as described for the KARE cohort subjects in the ACK-MC cohorts. The presence of OVF was identified using a standardised self-administered questionnaire and was confirmed by a radiological examination. Lateral radiographs of the thoracic and lumbar spines (T4 to L4) were obtained in all ACK-MC subjects. Vertebral fracture assessments were made in accordance with the recommendations of the Working Group on Vertebral Fractures.23 Radiographs were assessed by expert radiologists blinded to identifying information. AVF was defined quantitatively as >20% reduction in any measured vertebral height (ie, anterior, middle, or posterior).24 To clarify the absence of OF in the control group, we excluded not only subjects with OVF but also those with non-VF events using a standardised self-administered questionnaire.

In the RIKEN cohort, the case group consisted of 369 post-menopausal women (≥60 years of age) with OVF recruited from the BioBank Japan Project (http://biobankjp.org/). The control group consisted of 560 postmenopausal females (≥60 years of age) recruited from several medical institutes in Japan. The presence of OVF was also confirmed by a radiological examination and a standardised self-questionnaire. Diagnoses of VF were made by expert physicians who participated in the study. AVF was defined according to criteria of the Japanese Society for Bone and Mineral Research.25 A VF was defined as a ratio of the central height (C) to the anterior (A) or posterior (P) height of the vertebra of <0.8, or a ratio of A to P of <0.75; a crushed vertebra was recorded when the height (A, C, or P) was reduced by >20% compared with the adjacent vertebrae. The absence of OVF in the control population was not confirmed radiologically.

All studies were approved by the ethics review committees of the respective institutes and all participants provided written informed consent.

Genotyping and quality control

A total of 10 004 samples were genotyped using Affymetrix Genome-Wide Human Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) 5.0 arrays and processed with Bayesian Robust Linear Modelling using the Mahalanobis Distance genotyping algorithm. Samples that exhibited the following properties were excluded: genotyping calls <96% (n=401), heterozygosity >30% (n=11), sex inconsistency (n=41), or identity-by-state (IBS) value >0.80 (n=608). Markers with a high missing call rate (>5%), minor allele frequency (MAF) <1%, and significant deviation from the Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium (HWE) (p<1×10−6) were also excluded. The remaining 359 918 SNPs were used in subsequent analyses for association.18 We genotyped 18 discovery SNPs (rs7809764, rs3181225, rs2277730, rs3781551, rs4753784, rs1024896, rs12477614, rs11111767, rs9392697, rs868113, rs16965880, rs156928, rs784288, rs1569188, rs16841556, rs1509957, rs11751618, rs4768204) using the GoldenGate assay (Illumina Inc, San Diego, California, USA) in the Asan and Catholic Medical Center (ACMC) cohort. For one discovery SNP (rs10816453), we were not able to design a functioning assay on either platform. Genotyping accuracy was tested by the Illumina Gencall score and by checking for HWE. The Gencall score is a measure of the confidence of the clustering of individual SNP genotypes.

Genotyping in RIKEN was conducted using the multiplex-PCR invader assay26 or the TaqMan assay (Applied Biosystems). All cluster plots were checked by visual inspection, and SNPs with ambiguous calls were excluded.

Multidimensional scaling analysis and principal component analysis

To detect population substructures, we carried out multidimensional scaling (MDS) analysis and principal component analysis (PCA) using 44 724 SNP markers (see IBS analysis, below) among all KARE individuals and the 270 individuals in the HapMap Project. For MDS analysis, dimensions were calculated on the basis of the identity-by-descent pair-wise distance between all individuals in both KARE and HapMap groups using PLINK and R. The first dimension values were plotted against the second dimension values. For PCA, KARE and HapMap samples were plotted using the first two eigenvectors produced by the Smart PCA module in the EIGENSTRAT software package.18

IBS analysis

Pair-wise IBS between individuals was calculated using a subset of pruned markers (44 724 SNPs) that were in approximate linkage equilibrium. IBS analysis was done using PLINK software and R statistics packages. The calculated genome-wide average IBS between each pair of individuals was used to estimate individuals who are presumably in first degree relationships or relationships with more distant relatives whose clusters were tightly linked to the first degree relationships.18

SNP imputation

Imputation analysis was performed using IMPUTE against all of the HapMap Asian (JPT (Japanese in Tokyo) + CHB (Han Chinese in Beijing)) population (release 22/NCBI, build 36, and dbSNP build 126) for a total of 1 573 409 SNPs. We used posterior probability to call the genotype from imputation data and then performed association analyses on imputed data. We did not use allele dosages in the analyses to account for imputation uncertainty. Of these, SNPs in each cohort with a posterior probability score <0.90, high genotype information content (info <0.5),27 HWE (p<1×10−7), SNP missing rate >0.1, or MAF <0.01 were dropped.

Association analyses

Associations were analysed using the PLINK (http://pngu.mgh.harvard.edu/~purcell/plink/) and SAS programs (v.9.1; SAS Institute Inc, Cary, North Carolina, USA). Meta-analysis was performed on the combined results using effect size and SE estimated for each study. The associations between genotypes and OF risk were assessed in an additive model by logistic regression. In the ACK-MC and RIKEN cohorts, we analysed the associations after adjustments for age, weight, and height. The results of all cohorts were combined using the inverse variance meta-analysis method, assuming fixed effects. Cochran’s Q test was used to assess heterogeneity among the three studies. All meta-analysis calculations were performed using the R program (v.2.7.1). Associations between the BMD and SNPs were assessed by linear regression analysis in an additive model after adjustments for age, weight, and height.

Quantile–quantile plot

Under the null hypothesis of no association, the genome-wide p values of given SNPs should follow a uniform distribution. Quantile–quantile (Q–Q) plots of genome-wide p values were generated to detect SNPs that showed strong deviations from the null distribution because of strong associations with a related trait. The genomic inflation factor (λ) was estimated from the median of the χ2 statistic divided by 0.456, as suggested previously by Delvin and colleagues.28,29

Expression quantitative trait locus (eQTL) analysis

Gene expression information in the Epstein–Barr virus transformed lymphoblastoid cell lines (LCLs) of 210 individuals from HapMap phase II (60 from the CEU (Utah residents with northern and western European ancestry) population, 60 from the YRI (Yoruba in Ibadan, Nigeria) population, 45 from the CHB population, and 45 from the JPT population) was obtained from the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus.30 SNP genotype data were derived for each individual from the corresponding HapMap Phase II dataset.

RESULTS

In an attempt to detect the genetic susceptibility to OF in East Asian women, we first conducted a GWAS screen of an epidemiological cohort (KARE, n=1427), followed by a two stage de novo replication strategy (table 1). The Q–Q plot of the observed p values derived from the trend test showed a tendency to deviate from the null distribution in the tail (see online supplementary figure S1). The estimated value of the genomic control inflation factor (λ=1.007) indicated limited evidence of population stratification in the KARE study samples and supported the validity of ignoring stratification in our study samples (see online supplementary figure S1).

We identified 19 suggestively associated SNPs at p<5×10−4 in the discovery set (see online supplementary table S1 and online supplementary figure S2). As the first replication study, we tried to genotype the 19 SNPs in the ACK-MC cohort, but we were unable to design a functioning assay for one SNP (rs10816453). Therefore, we were able to analyse associations between 18 SNPs with OVF (see online supplementary table S2). However, 16 SNPs were found not to be associated with OVF in the cohort. Two SNPs (rs784288 of MECOM and rs1569188 of RYR2) were shown to be significantly associated with OVF (n=2207, p=2.00×10−3 of rs784288 and p=2.52×10−3 of rs1569188, respectively). The rs1569188 SNP in the RYR2 gene locus was excluded from further analysis as its associated direction was opposite to that in the discovery cohort. Finally, the rs784288 SNP in the MECOM gene, which was observed to have multiple diverse effects from text mining, was selected for further study. A further replication study revealed its consistent association with OVF in the RIKEN cohort also, an independent ethnic group (n=929, p=1.48×10−2) from Japan. An overall meta-analysis of the total samples (n=4563) yielded genome-wide significance (OR 1.39, 95% CI 1.24 to 1.56; p=3.59×10−8) for association with OF (table 2 and figure 1).

To identify a possible functional role of the newly identified variant, we performed expression quantitative trait locus (eQTL) analysis, examining associations between the rs784288 SNP and mRNA expression levels of the MECOM gene. The SNP rs784288 was significantly associated with MECOM mRNA expression levels in Epstein–Barr virus transformed lymphoblastoid cell lines of 210 HapMap phase II individuals of European, African and East Asian ancestry (peQTL=1.17×10−2). Comparative sequence analysis of the SNP locus (rs784288) among mammals showed a high degree of conservation, including a wide-spanning intron structure (see online supplementary figure S3).

MECOM is reported to be involved in hematopoietic differentiation and myelogenous leukemogenesis,31 and osteoclasts, the bone resorbing cells, are derived from haematopoietic progenitor cells of the monocyte–macrophage lineages. Therefore, we tested the role of MECOM in osteoclast biology to assess the function of the identified variants. Real-time PCR revealed that differentiated osteoclasts (19.9±1.9 fold higher than the control at 3 days of incubation) had higher MECOM expression than pre-osteoclasts (2.9±0.9 fold higher than the control at 3 days of incubation) (p<0.05) (figure 2A). Treatments with two siRNAs against MECOM significantly suppressed osteoclastogenesis (60.3±20.5, and 107.3±14.5, respectively), compared with control nonsense siRNA treated and scrambled siRNA treated groups (220.0±13.8 and 239.8±14.2, respectively) (all, p<0.05) (figure 2B,C). Real-time PCR further showed that a knockdown of MECOM suppresses the expression of marker genes for differentiated osteoclasts apart from integrin αv (0.46±0.1 fold for tartrate-resistant acid phosphatase (TRAP), 0.43±0.2 fold for cathepsin K, 0.56±0.1 fold for matrix metalloproteinase 9 (MMP9), and 0.68±0.1 fold for integrin β3), compared with the control siRNA treated group (all, p<0.05) (figure 2D). Western blot analysis gave similar results (figure 2E), suggesting that MECOM may stimulate osteoclastogenesis, resulting in increased bone resorption. In linear regression analyses, MECOM rs784288 correlated with decreased BMD values at the total femur (p=0.02) from the AMC and CMC cohorts (see online supplementary figure S4), consistent with its higher OF risk association.

DISCUSSION

We performed GWAS and follow-up replication analysis in East Asian populations, thereby identifying MECOM as a new susceptibility gene for OF. The SNP rs784288 is located on chromosome 3q26 within the MECOM gene, which encodes ecotropic viral integration site 1 and myelodysplasia syndrome 1 (figure 1).32 MECOM also has a role as a transcription factor33 involved in hematopoietic differentiation34 and myelogenous leukemogenesis.31 Considering the contribution of conserved functional elements within intronic sequences,35 comparative genomic analyses of highly conserved intronic sequences may provide valuable insights into the function of corresponding genes.36

The two major cells responsible for bone formation and resorption are osteoblasts (derived from bone marrow stromal cells) and osteoclasts (derived from hematopoietic progenitor cells), respectively. Because MECOM is reported to be involved in haematopoietic differentiation and myelogenous leukemogenesis,31 we tested its role in osteoclast biology. We noted that MECOM expression was higher in mature osteoclasts than in osteoclast precursors, suggesting a key role in osteoclastic biology. In addition, siRNA mediated MECOM knockdown suppressed osteoclastogenesis and the increased expression of TRAP, cathepsin K, MMP9 and integrin β3 by receptor activator of nuclear factor κ-B ligand. Although the mechanism linking MECOM to osteoclastogenesis has not been established, MECOM may act by regulating the RUNX1 gene. Modulation of bone resorption by MECOM could influence fracture risk by affecting both bone mass and bone quality. In pathway based association analysis, we observed a protein–protein interaction network involving osteoporosis related major candidate genes (see online supplementary figure S5). For example, MECOM shows strong connections to previously characterised osteoporosis genes such as RUNX1 and core binding factor subunit β, implying it has a physiological role in the regulation of haematopoietic development.37

To assess the functional relevance of MECOM further, we performed eQTL analysis of the association between rs784288 and the mRNA levels of nearby genes. Generally, an analysis of cis-eQTL in tissues directly relevant to the phenotype is more informative than from unrelated tissue types. However, recent reports have shown that cis-eQTLs are conserved across tissues, and that tissue specific genetic variations comprise only a small fraction of gene expression diversity.38,39 In the case of bone tissue, there is a large overlap in the transcriptomic effects of genetic variation between human LCLs and human osteo-blasts.40,41 Together, these observations suggest that expression of the MECOM gene in LCLs can provide supplementary insights that are indirectly informative for osteoporosis. Therefore, we further investigated cis-eQTL associations to ascertain whether the risk allele was associated with the allele specific expression pattern of the MECOM transcript in lymphoblastoid cell lines of 210 HapMap phase II individuals.30 Significant differences in expression were found to be associated with each increased copy of the risk allele. These results suggest that the higher MECOM transcript levels associated with the rs784288A allele is supporting evidence for cis-regulatory features.

Our study has limitations of the phenotypic specificity underlying BMD and/or OF variations, which were also shown in a recently published large meta-analysis.42 In linear regression analyses in our current study, MECOM rs784288 was found to be associated with decreased BMD in the total femur (p=0.02), consistent with its relationship with higher OF risk. However, despite a moderate effect on BMD variance, our meta-analysis revealed a genome-wide significant association (OR 1.39, 95% CI 1.24 to 1.56; p=3.59×10−8) with a large genetic effect on OF in postmenopausal women. It was suggested that a newly identified SNP, rs784288, was a BMD mediated, pathway independent risk factor for fracture, consistent with the results of studies showing a heritable component to fracture that is largely independent of the BMD.42,43 In the largest most recent meta-analysis,42 14 fracture risk related loci were selected from 56 loci associated with BMD values in Eurocentric populations, including both men and women, and a smaller number of individuals of East Asian ancestry in the total cohort. Hence, the 19 SNPs identified in our current study might not have been identified in this earlier large meta-analysis.42

To validate the association between the MECOM gene and OF risk, including sex and ethnic specificities, a further study in a larger cohort is needed to expand upon the Korean and Japanese postmenopausal women with OVF risk included in our present analysis. A second limitation is that we did not identify all variants of the MECOM gene in our cohorts to validate further their functional effects on bone metabolism and/or the risk of fracture. Despite the limited number of postmenopausal women in the AMC cohort (n=982) and discovery state, we recently performed targeted resequencing of the MECOM gene in the cases with OF (n=164) and controls (n=818) (see online supplementary data and online supplementary table S3). We identified 66 MECOM SNPs (data not shown), and a further three SNPs in genes associated with the risk of OF (see online supplementary table S4). Although this screen should be replicated in a larger cohort and further in vitro studies identifying their function are needed, we believe that these results provide additional evidence for the link between the MECOM gene and the risk of fracture.

Previous GWAS studies demonstrate that the MECOM gene has multiple diverse effects on phenotypic traits including aging,44 blood pressure,45 mean arterial pressure,46 serum magnesium concentration,47 lung (pulmonary) function,48 and nasopharyngeal carcinoma.49 To our knowledge, there have been no reports that the MECOM gene has a role in bone metabolism as a predisposing factor for OF. In addition, most previous OF associated GWAS signals are partly limited by genome-wide significant levels (see online supplementary table S5), and none has shown any functional relationship.

Taken together, our meta-analysis in East Asian individuals identifies a genome-wide significant association of the MECOM gene as a novel predisposing factor for OF, and further functional analysis suggests that this gene may have a functional role in bone metabolism. We also anticipate that identification of pleiotropic genetic effects between OF related phenotypes by incorporating a multivariate approach will lead to a better understanding of the pathogenesis of OF.
